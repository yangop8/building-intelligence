4.1.1From LM to LLM

Position: How Scaling transforms "language models" into "general reasoning systems"—history, key milestones, Scaling Law.

The Old Problem of Language Models

The definition of a language model has never changed: given the first $t-1$ tokens, predict the probability distribution of the $t$-th token.

$P(w_t | w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{t-1})$

From Shannon's information theory in 1948, to N-grams in the 1980s, to Bengio's neural language models in 2003, to Word2Vec and GloVe—for decades, NLP researchers have been finding better ways to model this conditional probability. But no one could have predicted that "stacking" this simple objective large enough would lead to emergent reasoning, programming, and mathematical abilities.

Key Breakthroughs

2017 Transformer: Google's "Attention is All You Need." Completely replaces RNNs, training is parallelizable, and results are superior. Foundation for everything that followed.

2018 BERT / GPT-1: Establishes the pre-training + fine-tuning paradigm. BERT uses masked language modeling for pre-training, dominating various downstream tasks; GPT uses causal language modeling, taking the generation route.

2020 GPT-3: 175B parameters, can perform tasks via few-shot learning without fine-tuning. "In-context learning" becomes the new paradigm—as models grow larger, they can learn new tasks from prompts. This is the turning point where LLM transforms from "feature extractor" to "general reasoner."

2022 ChatGPT: Brings RLHF to product grade; models can now "chat." Overnight, LLM transforms from a research topic to a consumer product.

2023 GPT-4 / Claude 2 / LLaMA-2: Multimodal, 100K+ context windows, and strong open-source models emerge. The ecosystem begins to bifurcate—closed-source commercial vs. open-weight.

2024 DeepSeek-V3 / o1 / Claude 3.5 / Qwen2.5: Open-source catches up with the first tier of closed-source models; "thinking at test time" (test-time compute) becomes a new dimension, allowing models to "think" for extended periods before output.

2025 DeepSeek-R1 / Qwen3 / LLaMA 4 / Claude 4 / GPT-5 / Gemini 3: Explosion of reasoning models. DeepSeek-R1 launches in early 2025, achieving GPT-4o-level reasoning via pure RL but at a tenth the cost, triggering a global open-source race; Qwen3 introduces a "thinking mode" toggle, covering both reasoning and non-reasoning scenarios in a single model; DeepSeek's subsequent V3.1 unifies thinking/non-thinking modes, while V3.2 introduces Sparse Attention (DSA) to further reduce costs; LLaMA 4 family (Scout/Maverick) uses MoE for the first time; Claude 4/4.5 emphasizes agent stability, while OpenAI GPT-5 and Google Gemini 3 compete for top reasoning benchmarks.

Early 2026: Open-source MoE densifies further—Qwen3-Next 80B-A3B, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B, Kimi K2, GLM-4.5/4.6, and MiniMax-M2 all emerge from late 2025 through early 2026. On the closed-source side, OpenAI releases GPT-4.2 (2025.12), followed by GPT-4.4 on 2026.3.5; the Claude series evolves to Opus 4.6 / Sonnet 4.6 / Haiku 4.5. Anthropic's Claude Mythos Preview in 2026.4 is a critical milestone—this unreleased frontier model exhibits "emergent-level" abilities in cybersecurity, automatically discovering thousands of zero-day vulnerabilities in mainstream operating systems and browsers. Anthropic makes an exception by not releasing it publicly, instead providing it exclusively to 50+ major enterprise partners through Project Glasswing for defensive purposes. This is the first time a major AI company has proactively constrained a frontier model's release for safety reasons since OpenAI's restricted GPT-2 release in 2019.

In March 2026, DeepSeek releases V4 (next-generation flagship) and R2 (R1's successor reasoning model with significantly improved multilingual reasoning); Alibaba releases Qwen3.6-Plus Preview, supporting 1M context and always-on CoT reasoning. Major disruption in video generation: OpenAI announces on 2026.3.24 the shutdown of the entire Sora product line (including standalone app and API), redirecting resources to robot world simulation research; meanwhile, Kling 3.0 becomes the first natively 4K 60fps video generation model.

The gap between open-source and closed-source narrows further to 3-6 months. Anthropic's MCP (Model Context Protocol) becomes the standard for Agent tool calling throughout 2025, widely adopted across Claude, Cursor, and the VSCode ecosystem. In 2025.12, MCP is donated to the Linux Foundation's Agentic AI Foundation (jointly founded by Anthropic, Block, and OpenAI), becoming the industry-standard protocol.

Scaling Law: The Mathematical Explanation for All This

Why does scaling up enable emergent abilities? Two papers provide the answer: Kaplan 2020 and Chinchilla 2022. Loss is a power-law function of parameter count $N$, data quantity $D$, and compute budget $C$.

$L(N, D) \approx E + \frac{A}{N^\alpha} + \frac{B}{D^\beta}$

$E$ is the irreducible entropy of the data (theoretical lower bound), and $A/N^\alpha, B/D^\beta$ are compressible portions. The power law means that as long as compute continues to scale, loss continues to decrease—no saturation has been observed.

More intriguingly, Chinchilla's finding: given a compute budget $C = 6ND$, the optimal point is when $N$ and $D$ grow in roughly equal proportion (with a specific ratio of about 1:20 tokens/param). GPT-3's 175B parameters actually didn't have enough training data (300B tokens); Chinchilla proved that 70B parameters with 1.4T tokens performs better. This guided the design of the LLaMA family.

Emergent Abilities

Scaling is not linear—certain abilities suddenly appear when model size crosses a threshold (in-context learning, CoT reasoning, multi-step math). This is called "emergent abilities." Small models can't learn from limited prompts; large models master a task from just two or three examples.

Some 2023 work argued that emergence is an illusion of metric choice (BIG-Bench uses strict accuracy, appearing to "emerge" as it jumps from 0 to non-zero; switching to log-likelihood smooths it out). But even so, the phenomenon remains: there are real qualitative differences in capability between large and small models.

From Scaling to Test-Time Compute

In late 2024, the rules of the game changed again. OpenAI's o1 demonstrated that models don't just scale during training—test-time scaling also offers enormous gains. Letting models "think" for extended periods (generating many reasoning tokens before outputting an answer) can double performance. OpenAI subsequently released o3 and o3-mini, further boosting reasoning—o3 achieves breakthrough results (87.5%) on the ARC-AGI benchmark, exceeding the human average for the first time. DeepSeek-R1, QwQ, and Claude 3.7 Sonnet's thinking mode all follow this path.

Throughout 2025, this direction dominated LLM evolution. Key developments:

  • DeepSeek-R1 (2025.1): An open-source reasoning model trained via pure RL (GRPO algorithm) without requiring human annotations of reasoning traces—overturning the assumption that "alignment data is essential." The R1-0528 version exceeds GPT-4o on AIME math problems.
  • Hybrid thinking/non-thinking modes: Qwen3 pioneers a "mode toggle"—users flip a switch to enable or disable extended reasoning. DeepSeek V3.1 further merges both modes into one model, with chat templates controlling behavior. This design became standard for open-source models in the second half of 2025.
  • Thinking budget: The product side gradually exposes a "thinking budget" parameter, letting users control reasoning length—the same question can be answered with 1K tokens of thinking (fast) or 16K tokens (deep).

Test-time compute transforms "one forward pass determines capability" into "pay more compute for better answers"—a significant shift for the product side. This is one of the major axes of LLM evolution in the coming years.

4.2.1Overall Structure and Information Flow

Position: The complete path of a token from model entry to output — establishing the macro picture.

Overall Flow

From user input "Hello world" to the model predicting the next token, the steps are:

flowchart TB INP[User Input Text] --> TOK[Tokenization] TOK --> EMB[Token Embedding] EMB --> POS[+ Position Encoding] POS --> L1[Transformer Block x N] L1 --> NORM[Final LayerNorm/RMSNorm] NORM --> LMH[LM Head Linear] LMH --> SOFT[Softmax] SOFT --> SAM[Sample next token]

Each Transformer Block is a repeated unit of the same structure. GPT-3 has 96, LLaMA-3-70B has 80. Each block does two things:

  1. Self-Attention: lets each position "see" other positions, enabling lateral information exchange
  2. FFN (Feed-Forward Network): independently transforms each position, enhancing expressiveness

Each operation is wrapped in "normalization + residual connection." Modern LLMs use Pre-Norm — normalization before the residual. The original Transformer used Post-Norm (normalize after residual), which is less stable.

Shape Evolution

Tracking a token ID tensor of shape [B, L] (B=batch, L=seq len) through the model:

StageShape
Input token IDs[B, L]
After Embedding[B, L, d_model]
Inside each attention layer[B, H, L, d_head] (H heads, d_head = d_model/H)
Attention layer output[B, L, d_model]
Inside FFN[B, L, d_ff] (d_ff is typically 4×d_model)
Final output[B, L, vocab_size]

Parameter Distribution

The parameters of a Transformer Block are mainly in:

  • Attention's four linear layers Q/K/V/O: $4 \times d_{model}^2$
  • FFN's two linear layers: $2 \times d_{model} \times d_{ff} = 8 \times d_{model}^2$ (with $d_{ff} = 4 d_{model}$)

So FFN accounts for 2/3 of the parameters per layer, attention accounts for 1/3. This motivates MoE — sparsifying the large FFN significantly reduces computation.

Total model parameters $\approx 12 \times N_{layers} \times d_{model}^2$ + embedding table ($V \times d_{model}$, V = vocabulary size). LLaMA-3-70B has 80 layers, d_model=8192, giving ≈ 64B, plus embedding and miscellaneous ≈ 70B.

Forward and Backward Pass

During training, given the entire sequence, one forward pass produces the predicted distribution for every position. The loss is cross-entropy computed at every position (predicting the next token), summed and then backpropagated. One forward + backward pass costs approximately $6 \times N_{params} \times N_{tokens}$ FLOPs.

During inference, starting from a prompt, only the next token is predicted each time. See Section 4.8 for details.

4.2.2Self-Attention: Complete Derivation

Position: The soul operator of LLMs — deriving every detail, including why we divide by √d.

Problem Setup

Input: $L$ tokens, each a $d$-dimensional vector $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Goal: make the output $\mathbf{y}_i$ at each position $i$ a dynamic weighted combination of all input positions, with weights determined by the "similarity" between $\mathbf{x}_i$ and other positions.

The Roles of Query / Key / Value

Core idea: each position plays three roles:

  • Query: $\mathbf{q}_i = W_Q \mathbf{x}_i$ — "what kind of information am I looking for"
  • Key: $\mathbf{k}_i = W_K \mathbf{x}_i$ — "what kind of information am I"
  • Value: $\mathbf{v}_i = W_V \mathbf{x}_i$ — "what am I contributing"

When computing the output for position $i$: compare $\mathbf{q}_i$ against all $\mathbf{k}_j$ to get weights $\alpha_{ij}$, then use $\alpha_{ij}$ to compute a weighted average over all $\mathbf{v}_j$. This three-way separation gives the model expressiveness — the relevance of "i to j" and "j to i" can differ.

Matrix Derivation

Stack all tokens: $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$. Compute:

$\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{X} W_Q, \quad \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{X} W_K, \quad \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{X} W_V$

These are all $L \times d$ matrices ($W_*$ are $d \times d$ projections). Then compute similarity:

$\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{Q} \mathbf{K}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{L \times L}$

$S_{ij}$ is the dot product of query $i$ and key $j$ — larger values indicate greater "similarity."

Why Divide by √d

There is a critical detail here. $\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j = \sum_{k=1}^d q_k \cdot k_k$. If each dimension of $q$ and $k$ is independent with mean 0 and variance 1, the variance of the dot product is $d$ (standard deviation $\sqrt{d}$).

When $d$ is large, the range of dot products is wide — some very large, some very small. When fed into softmax, softmax is dominated by the maximum value (the "sharpening" effect of softmax). If $s_1 = 30, s_2 = 25$, a difference of 5 causes softmax to almost exclusively attend to $s_1$. Even worse: gradients vanish — softmax has near-zero gradients in the "winner-takes-all" regime.

The original Transformer paper's fix: divide by $\sqrt{d_k}$ to bring the dot product variance back to 1:

$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q} \mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) \mathbf{V}$

This is "Scaled Dot-Product Attention."

Causal Mask

LLMs are causal (each position can only see itself and prior positions), so the upper triangle of $\mathbf{S}$ is set to $-\infty$, which becomes 0 after softmax:

import torch

def causal_attention(Q, K, V):
    # Q, K, V: [B, H, L, d_head]
    d_k = Q.size(-1)
    S = Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1) / d_k ** 0.5   # [B, H, L, L]
    L = S.size(-1)
    mask = torch.triu(torch.ones(L, L, dtype=torch.bool), diagonal=1)
    S = S.masked_fill(mask, float('-inf'))
    A = torch.softmax(S, dim=-1)
    return A @ V   # [B, H, L, d_head]

Computational Complexity

Computing $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top$: $O(L^2 d)$. Softmax: $O(L^2)$. Multiply by $\mathbf{V}$: $O(L^2 d)$. Total: $O(L^2 d)$.

Memory: the $\mathbf{S}$ matrix is $O(L^2)$; at $L=4096$, that's 16M elements × FP16 = 32MB per head per sample. This is the "quadratic complexity bottleneck" of attention — the fundamental challenge for long contexts. FlashAttention, sparse attention, and Mamba all address this.

4.2.3Multi-Head Attention

Position: Splitting attention into multiple heads — why it works and how to implement it.

Motivation

Single-head attention can only learn one "attention pattern" — it might learn coreference, or syntactic proximity, but a single head can only represent one. Multiple heads let the model learn multiple patterns simultaneously: each head has independent $W_Q^h, W_K^h, W_V^h$ projections that learn different patterns.

Implementation

A naive implementation runs $H$ separate attention passes and concatenates. In practice, for efficiency, one large projection is used and split into heads:

  1. Project $\mathbf{X}$ to $d_{model}$-dimensional Q/K/V
  2. Reshape into $H$ subspaces of $d_{head}$ dimensions each: $d_{model} = H \times d_{head}$
  3. Each head independently performs attention
  4. Concatenate back to $d_{model}$ dimensions, then apply output projection $W_O$
import torch
import torch.nn as nn

class MultiHeadAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads):
        super().__init__()
        assert d_model % n_heads == 0
        self.H = n_heads
        self.d_head = d_model // n_heads
        self.Wq = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.Wk = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.Wv = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.Wo = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x, mask=None):
        B, L, D = x.shape
        Q = self.Wq(x).view(B, L, self.H, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        K = self.Wk(x).view(B, L, self.H, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        V = self.Wv(x).view(B, L, self.H, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        S = Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1) / self.d_head ** 0.5
        if mask is not None: S = S.masked_fill(mask, float('-inf'))
        A = torch.softmax(S, dim=-1)
        out = A @ V
        out = out.transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, L, D)
        return self.Wo(out)

4.2.4FFN, Residual Connections, and Normalization

Position: The other half of the Transformer Block — FFN enhances expressiveness, residual connections and normalization ensure trainability.

The Role of FFN

Attention enables communication between positions, but attention output remains in the linear subspace of token representations — attention alone limits model expressiveness. FFN independently applies a nonlinear transformation at each position, projecting the representation into a high-dimensional nonlinear space and back.

$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{x}) = W_2 \cdot \text{activation}(W_1 \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_1) + \mathbf{b}_2$

$W_1$ maps $d_{model} \to d_{ff}$ (expanding), $W_2$ maps $d_{ff} \to d_{model}$ (contracting). $d_{ff}$ is typically 4× $d_{model}$.

Activation Function Evolution

Original Transformer: ReLU. BERT/GPT era: GELU. LLaMA era: SwiGLU.

The Mechanism of SwiGLU

SwiGLU is not an ordinary activation function but a "gated linear unit." It modifies the FFN to:

$\text{SwiGLU}(\mathbf{x}) = W_3 \cdot \left( \text{SiLU}(W_1 \mathbf{x}) \odot (W_2 \mathbf{x}) \right)$

Three linear projections $W_1, W_2, W_3$ are used. $W_1$ goes through SiLU, $W_2$ does not, and the two are multiplied element-wise. $W_3$ projects the result back to $d_{model}$.

The element-wise product $(W_2 \mathbf{x})$ acts as a "gate" — it determines which dimensions pass through and which are suppressed. More expressive than a plain MLP.

Parameter control: to keep the total parameter count equivalent to a 4× FFN, the hidden dimension is changed from $4d$ to $\frac{8}{3}d$. LLaMA, Qwen, Mistral, and DeepSeek all do this.

class SwiGLU(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, d_ff=None):
        super().__init__()
        d_ff = d_ff or int(8 * d_model / 3)
        self.W1 = nn.Linear(d_model, d_ff, bias=False)
        self.W2 = nn.Linear(d_model, d_ff, bias=False)
        self.W3 = nn.Linear(d_ff, d_model, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.W3(nn.functional.silu(self.W1(x)) * self.W2(x))

Residual Connections

Chapter 3 Section 4.4.1 covered ResNet's residual idea. Transformer uses it too: each sublayer's (attention and FFN) output is added to its input. $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x} + \text{Sublayer}(\mathbf{x})$.

Benefits: gradients flow through the residual path directly to deeper layers, preventing vanishing gradients; if a layer doesn't learn a useful transformation, the residual keeps it close to identity mapping without degrading performance; enables training 80+ layer models.

Pre-Norm vs Post-Norm

Where normalization is placed matters.

Post-Norm (original Transformer): y = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x)). Normalize after residual addition.

Pre-Norm (since GPT-2): y = x + Sublayer(LayerNorm(x)). Normalize before the sublayer; residual is added directly to the input.

Pre-Norm wins on training stability: the residual path is "clean," allowing gradients to flow unimpeded. Post-Norm's residual path passes through LayerNorm, scaling the gradients and making deep networks harder to train.

Trade-off: Pre-Norm's final output variance accumulates across layers (one residual addition per layer), so Pre-Norm models need a final LayerNorm at the end. All modern LLMs use Pre-Norm.

RMSNorm as a Replacement for LayerNorm

Mentioned in Chapter 3 Section 4.3.5. LayerNorm: $\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma} \gamma + \beta$, requiring mean, variance, and bias computations.

RMSNorm simplifies: divide only by the root mean square, no mean subtraction, no bias.

$\text{RMSNorm}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{d}\sum_i x_i^2 + \epsilon}} \odot \boldsymbol{\gamma}$

Empirically performs similarly to LayerNorm, but faster (saves one mean computation and one subtraction) and has fewer parameters (no $\beta$). LLaMA uses RMSNorm, and most subsequent models have followed suit.

4.2.5Encoder/Decoder Divide

Position: Three Transformer variants — Encoder-only, Decoder-only, Encoder-Decoder — and where each is used.

Three Architectures

The original Transformer (2017) is Encoder-Decoder — designed for translation tasks: the encoder reads the source language, and the decoder attends to the encoder output and generates the target language. Three lines evolved from this.

Encoder-Only (BERT Family)

Only an encoder stack, with bidirectional attention (each position sees all positions). Training objective: MLM (Masked Language Modeling) — mask 15% of tokens and have the model predict them.

Strength: top-tier for understanding tasks (classification, NER, sentence similarity), because every position's representation has full context.

Representatives: BERT (2018), RoBERTa, DeBERTa, Electra. Still widely used today as "text encoders" — most embedding models for sentence embeddings and RAG are in this family.

Decoder-Only (GPT Family)

Only a decoder stack, with unidirectional attention (causal mask — each position only sees itself and prior positions). Training objective: CLM (Causal LM) — predict the next token.

Strength: naturally fits generation. Also, emergent abilities like in-context learning and few-shot learning appear mainly in decoder-only large models.

Representatives: GPT series, LLaMA, Qwen, DeepSeek, Mistral — essentially all modern LLMs.

Encoder-Decoder (T5/BART)

Full encoder-decoder architecture. Diverse training objectives: T5 uses span corruption (mask a span and generate it); BART uses denoising (recover from various corruptions).

Strength: tasks with clear input-output separation (translation, summarization, Q&A) — encoder focuses on understanding, decoder focuses on generation.

Representatives: T5 (Google), BART, mT5, FLAN-T5. Today's mainstream LLMs have largely abandoned this route — decoder-only is simpler and scales better.

Why Decoder-Only Won

  1. Simpler training: one objective (next token), all text is training data. Encoder-Decoder requires designing input-output pairs.
  2. In-context learning: put task instructions and examples in the prompt, the model self-adapts — no specialized architecture design needed.
  3. Generation capability: even for understanding tasks, having decoder-only write out an answer works (and is often stronger, because it can do CoT).
  4. Scaling behavior: empirically, decoder-only responds best to parameter increases.

Encoder-only remains active in scenarios that require embeddings — because decoder-only doesn't naturally produce "sentence-level representations" (one must approximate using the last token or average pooling). But for generation tasks, decoder-only dominates.

PART THREE

4.3 Positional Encoding and Long Context

Attention itself has no notion of order — positional encoding provides it. Long contexts require positional encoding to "extrapolate."

4.3.1History of Positional Encoding

Position: From sinusoidal absolute encoding, to learned, to relative positional encoding, to RoPE — the evolutionary logic.

Why Positional Encoding Is Needed

Attention is a "set operation" — if you permute the input token order, the attention output for each position permutes accordingly, but semantically each position's representation is unchanged. "The cat sat on the mat" and "mat the on sat cat The" would produce identical intermediate representations. Clearly wrong. So positional information must be injected at each position.

Sinusoidal Absolute Encoding (Original Transformer)

Assign each position a $d$-dimensional vector using sinusoids at different frequencies:

$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin(\text{pos} / 10000^{2i/d})$
$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1) = \cos(\text{pos} / 10000^{2i/d})$

Added to the token embedding. Advantage: no parameters, and has some "guessing" ability when extrapolating beyond training length (because it's a continuous function). Problem: extrapolation is actually terrible in practice — a model trained to position 2048 breaks at position 2049.

Learned Positional Encoding

GPT-2 simplified: give each position a learnable embedding. Supports whatever length it was trained on — exceeding that limit breaks immediately. More expressive within the training window but completely unable to extrapolate.

Relative Positional Encoding

A shift in approach: what truly matters is not the absolute position "word #5" but the relative distance "how many words apart."

T5's approach: add a bias $b_{i-j}$ to the attention score, where $b$ is learnable. The same bias is used for the same distance. ALiBi (2022) is more aggressive: use a fixed linear decay $-m \cdot |i - j|$ as a bias with no learnable parameters. ALiBi trained to 1024 length can extrapolate to 4096+.

RoPE

Proposed by RoFormer (2021). Today almost all mainstream models (LLaMA, Qwen, DeepSeek, Mistral) use it. Deep dive in the next section.

4.3.2RoPE Deep Dive

Position: Rotary positional encoding — mathematically elegant, simple to implement, the de facto standard.

Core Idea

RoPE does not add a position vector to the input; instead, it rotates Q and K — so that $\mathbf{q}_m \cdot \mathbf{k}_n$ naturally encodes the relative position $m - n$.

Two-Dimensional Case

Start with the 2D case. Treat each pair of adjacent dimensions of Q and K as a complex number (or 2D plane vector). For position $m$, rotate $\mathbf{q}_m$ by angle $m\theta$:

$\begin{pmatrix} q_m^{(1)} \\ q_m^{(2)} \end{pmatrix} \to \begin{pmatrix} \cos m\theta & -\sin m\theta \\ \sin m\theta & \cos m\theta \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} q_m^{(1)} \\ q_m^{(2)} \end{pmatrix}$

Similarly rotate $\mathbf{k}_n$ by angle $n\theta$. Then compute the dot product:

$\mathbf{q}_m^{\text{RoPE}} \cdot \mathbf{k}_n^{\text{RoPE}} = f(m - n)$

Depends only on $m - n$! This is exactly the property that relative positional encoding seeks.

Intuition: treat the vectors as complex numbers $q_m, k_n$. Rotating by $m\theta$ is equivalent to multiplying by $e^{im\theta}$. The dot product becomes $q_m e^{im\theta} \cdot (k_n e^{in\theta})^* = q_m k_n^* e^{i(m-n)\theta}$ — depends only on $m-n$.

High Dimensions: Pairwise Rotation

For $d$-dimensional Q and K vectors, pair up dimensions two-by-two, each pair independently performs a 2D rotation, but with different frequencies:

$\theta_i = 10000^{-2(i-1)/d}, \quad i = 1, \ldots, d/2$

Low-index dimensions use high frequency ($\theta_1 = 1$, rotating 1 radian per step); high-index dimensions use low frequency ($\theta_{d/2} \approx 10000^{-1}$, rotating very little per step). This lets different dimensions capture positional differences at different scales.

Implementation

import torch

def build_rope_freqs(d_head, max_seq_len, base=10000):
    freqs = 1.0 / (base ** (torch.arange(0, d_head, 2).float() / d_head))
    t = torch.arange(max_seq_len).float()
    freqs_cis = torch.outer(t, freqs)
    return torch.polar(torch.ones_like(freqs_cis), freqs_cis)

def apply_rope(x, freqs_cis):
    x_complex = torch.view_as_complex(x.float().reshape(*x.shape[:-1], -1, 2))
    x_out = x_complex * freqs_cis[:x.size(-2)]
    return torch.view_as_real(x_out).flatten(-2).type_as(x)

Q = apply_rope(Q, freqs_cis)
K = apply_rope(K, freqs_cis)

Some details: RoPE only applies to Q and K, not V — V carries content information and shouldn't carry position; freqs_cis can be precomputed and cached, with zero overhead during inference.

Three Key Advantages of RoPE

  1. Relative position: naturally encodes distance rather than absolute position
  2. Decay property: inner products naturally decay with distance — matching the intuition that "distant words have weaker associations"
  3. Extrapolation potential: unseen positions can be extrapolated — continuous trigonometric functions don't suddenly break (though significant extrapolation still needs YaRN and similar techniques)

4.3.3Long Context Extrapolation

Position: How to use a model trained on 4K context for 128K — PI, NTK-aware, YaRN, LongRoPE.

The Problem

Training seen positions 0 to 4K; at inference a 64K sequence arrives — high-frequency rotation angles have cycled through millions of radians at position 64K, a rotation state the model has never seen, causing performance collapse.

PI: Position Interpolation

Proposed by Meta in 2023. The idea is straightforward: compress inference-time positions back into the training range. If training length is $L_{\text{train}}$ and target length is $L_{\text{new}}$, map position $m$ to $m \cdot L_{\text{train}} / L_{\text{new}}$.

Mathematically equivalent to reducing RoPE rotation frequencies by a factor of $L_{\text{new}}/L_{\text{train}}$. Downside: high-frequency information becomes blurry; the model needs slight fine-tuning (a few hundred steps) to adapt.

NTK-aware Scaling

PI compresses all dimensions equally. NTK-aware (community-proposed in 2023) observed: low-frequency dimensions shouldn't be compressed (they haven't completed a single cycle yet); only compress high frequencies.

Concretely: increase RoPE's base from 10000 to a larger value (e.g., 40000). This makes all frequencies smaller, but affects originally-small low frequencies less — precisely preserving large-scale information.

Benefit: 2-4× extrapolation without fine-tuning, with minimal quality degradation.

YaRN

Proposed by Nous Research in 2023 (Yet another RoPE extensioN). Builds on NTK-aware with finer adjustments: divides dimensions into three tiers by frequency (high-frequency unchanged, mid-frequency NTK scaling, low-frequency PI interpolation); adds temperature scaling to compensate for variance changes from length variation.

YaRN with minimal fine-tuning (~1B token scale) can extend LLaMA-2 from 4K to 128K with minimal quality loss. The mainstream approach in the open-source community today.

LongRoPE

Proposed by Microsoft in 2024, an upgrade to YaRN. Core innovation: rather than using the same scaling factor for all RoPE dimensions, search for the optimal per-dimension scaling — some dimensions compressed more, others less. Significant search cost, but the payoff: feasible on LLaMA-3 at 2M tokens.

Native Long-Context Training

Extrapolation is the easy approach, but training from scratch is better. Major labs increasingly train directly on long documents:

  • LLaMA-3: 8K pretraining, then extended to 128K using 800M tokens
  • Qwen2.5-1M: extended from 32K to 256K/1M using staged long-document training
  • Gemini 1.5: claims 1M or even 10M context; technical details not public

The Practical Meaning of Context Length

A model "supporting" 128K does not mean it "uses" 128K well. The Lost in the Middle phenomenon: information in the middle of long contexts tends to be ignored by the model. Evaluation benchmarks: Needle in a Haystack (hide a key sentence in a long document and ask the model to find it); RULER (comprehensive multi-task long-context evaluation); LongBench. Many models claiming 100K context see accuracy plummet beyond 50K. Real-world engineering requires testing before trusting claims.

PART FOUR

4.4 Attention Optimization Variants

The quadratic complexity of attention is LLM's greatest bottleneck — the evolution from MHA to MLA to Mamba.

4.4.1MQA / GQA / MLA

Position: Successive generations of solutions for reducing KV Cache — from MQA to DeepSeek's MLA.

Why KV Cache Is the Bottleneck

During inference, generating each new token requires computing the new token's Q, K, V; appending the new K, V to the existing cache; and having the new Q perform attention over all K, V. Recomputing all prior K, V each time costs $O(L^2)$ computation. KV Cache caches previously computed K, V so a new token only computes its own share, bringing total computation to $O(L)$.

But KV Cache consumes GPU memory. Estimate: 2 × layers × H × d_head × seq_len × 2 bytes (FP16). LLaMA-3-70B at L=8K requires KV Cache ≈ 2.5 GB per sample. At batch=10 that's 25GB — more than the model weights. At long contexts, KV Cache is the primary memory killer.

MHA / MQA / GQA

Standard MHA: $H$ heads with independent Q, K, V per head. KV Cache stores $H$ sets of K and $H$ sets of V. This is the baseline.

MQA (Noam Shazeer 2019): the extreme — all heads share a single K, V; only Q is independent per head. KV Cache shrinks by $H$×! But quality degrades noticeably — a single K, V lacks expressiveness. Used by PaLM and Falcon.

GQA: a compromise between MHA and MQA. $H$ heads divided into $G$ groups, each group sharing one K, V. When $G = H$ it degrades to MHA; when $G = 1$ it degrades to MQA. LLaMA-3-70B uses $G = 8$ (64 heads sharing 8 K, V groups), shrinking KV Cache by 8× with almost no quality loss.

Today LLaMA, Qwen, and Mistral all use GQA.

class GroupedQueryAttention(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_heads=64, n_kv_heads=8):
        super().__init__()
        self.H = n_heads
        self.G = n_kv_heads
        self.d_head = d_model // n_heads
        self.Wq = nn.Linear(d_model, n_heads * self.d_head, bias=False)
        self.Wk = nn.Linear(d_model, n_kv_heads * self.d_head, bias=False)
        self.Wv = nn.Linear(d_model, n_kv_heads * self.d_head, bias=False)
        self.Wo = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)

    def forward(self, x):
        B, L, _ = x.shape
        Q = self.Wq(x).view(B, L, self.H, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        K = self.Wk(x).view(B, L, self.G, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        V = self.Wv(x).view(B, L, self.G, self.d_head).transpose(1, 2)
        K = K.repeat_interleave(self.H // self.G, dim=1)
        V = V.repeat_interleave(self.H // self.G, dim=1)
        S = Q @ K.transpose(-2, -1) / self.d_head ** 0.5
        A = torch.softmax(S, dim=-1)
        out = (A @ V).transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, L, -1)
        return self.Wo(out)

MLA: DeepSeek's Innovation

DeepSeek-V2 introduced MLA (Multi-head Latent Attention), which further compresses KV Cache.

Core idea: instead of storing K and V, only store their low-rank latent representations. Specifically:

$\mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}} = W_{DKV} \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_c}$

$d_c \ll d_{model}$. This compressed vector $\mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}}$ is stored in the cache. When K, V are needed, recover them via up-projection:

$\mathbf{k} = W_{UK} \mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}}, \quad \mathbf{v} = W_{UV} \mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}}$

Analogous to LoRA — using low-rank decomposition to compress matrices. But MLA's elegance is: the up-projection matrix $W_{UK}$ can be merged with Q's projection, so no actual up-projection is needed during inference.

Specifically, the attention score $\mathbf{q}^\top \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{q}^\top W_{UK} \mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}} = (\mathbf{q}^\top W_{UK}) \mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}}$. Absorb $W_{UK}$ into Q's projection upfront; at inference time compute $\tilde{\mathbf{q}}^\top \mathbf{c}_{\text{kv}}$ directly — KV Cache only contains the compressed vectors.

DeepSeek-V2 uses $d_c = 4 d_{head}$, shrinking KV Cache by 90%+ compared to equivalent MHA, with even better quality (low-rank decomposition acts as a form of regularization).

MLA's Handling of RoPE

MLA has one awkward point: applying RoPE to K breaks the math of "merging matrices." DeepSeek's solution is to split K into two halves — a portion without positional information (goes through MLA compression) + a small portion with RoPE (not compressed, stored normally). This half/half split is an engineering compromise.

DSA: DeepSeek Sparse Attention (Introduced in V3.2)

DeepSeek's V3.2, released in October 2025, introduced a further innovation — Sparse Attention (DSA). Motivation: in long-context settings, MLA has already compressed KV Cache very small, but attention itself remains $O(L^2)$ computation. DSA adds "fine-grained sparsity" on top of MLA — each Q only attends to an important subset of K, rather than all of them.

Key innovations:

  1. Dynamic top-k selection: for each query, use a lightweight scoring function to find the most relevant small set of K; only perform attention on these K. Different queries select different K.
  2. Seamless integration with MLA: sparse + low-rank compression together reduce long-context compute cost by ~50% with almost no quality loss.
  3. Unified thinking/non-thinking: V3.2 inherits V3.1's hybrid mode — the same model can answer quickly or reason deeply.

DSA is the first time, after years of sparse attention research, that "quality matches dense attention with significantly lower cost" has been achieved in a large model. This also shows that the sparse attention direction covered in 4.4.2 hasn't failed — previous approaches just weren't fine-grained enough.

V3.2 also has a "Speciale" variant that pushes reasoning capability to reportedly near the level of top closed-source models — the open-source benchmark at the end of 2025.

4.4.2Sparse Attention

Position: Abandoning "every position attends to all positions," attending only to a key subset — replacing $O(L^2)$ with $O(L \log L)$ or even $O(L)$.

The Idea

Most weights in full attention are actually small — most position pairs have weak associations. Sparse attention computes selectively, saving time and memory.

Sliding Window

Simplest approach: each position only attends to neighbors within a window of size $w$. Complexity $O(L w)$; linear when $w$ is fixed. Used by Longformer, BigBird, Mistral (window 4096). Problem: distant information can't reach. Solution: after stacking multiple layers, the receptive field grows (layer $k$ has receptive field $k w$).

Dilated Attention

Strided attention — attend to every $d$-th position. Similar to dilated convolution. After a few layers, covers a large range.

Global Token

Designate a few "global tokens" (e.g., [CLS]) that can attend to all positions and all positions can attend to them. Other positions only attend locally. Global tokens serve as "information aggregation points." BigBird combines Sliding Window + Dilated + Global, providing both local detail and long-range bridging.

Routing Attention

Dynamically select which positions to attend to. Reformer uses LSH (Locality-Sensitive Hashing) to find similar Q, K pairs; Routing Transformer uses clustering. These methods are effective on some tasks but complex to implement.

Sliding Window + Attention Sink

StreamingLLM (2023) observed an interesting phenomenon: the initial few tokens (attention sinks) are crucial for maintaining stable attention distributions — even if semantically unimportant, the model assigns them large attention weights (acting as normalization).

For streaming scenarios: retain initial few tokens + recent window + discard the middle — the model can handle arbitrarily long inputs without degradation. Practically useful.

Why Sparse Attention Hasn't Dominated

Despite theoretical elegance, sparse attention in practice has not replaced full attention in large models. Reasons:

  1. FlashAttention optimizes full attention's constants to the extreme; the practical impact of $L^2$ complexity is less severe than theory suggests
  2. Capability gains from model scaling far exceed efficiency from sparse attention
  3. Hard-coded sparse patterns impose inductive biases that may not suit all tasks

The typical hybrid approach in today's open-source models is Mistral's "Sliding Window + full attention" — sliding in shallow layers, full attention in the last few layers — saving memory without sacrificing expressiveness.

4.4.3Linear Attention and Mamba

Position: The resurgence of RNNs — replacing KV Cache with "state," $O(L)$ inference, challenging Transformer.

The Linear Attention Idea

Standard attention: $\text{softmax}(QK^\top) V$. If softmax can be removed, this can be rewritten as:

$\text{LinAttn}(Q, K, V) = Q (K^\top V)$

Complexity: $K^\top V$ is $(d \times L)(L \times d) = d \times d$, $O(L d^2)$. Then $Q (K^\top V)$ is $O(L d^2)$. Total $O(L d^2)$ — linear!

But softmax can't simply be dropped — it ensures weight normalization and stability. Removing it consistently degrades quality. Various linear attention works (Linformer, Performer, Linear Transformer) propose different "softmax approximation" schemes, but none match full attention quality.

SSM: State Space Models

An idea borrowed from control theory. A linear state space system:

$\mathbf{h}_t = A \mathbf{h}_{t-1} + B \mathbf{x}_t, \quad \mathbf{y}_t = C \mathbf{h}_t$

$\mathbf{h}_t$ is the "state" — a fixed-size vector that stores historical information. This is an RNN structure.

Early SSM work (S4, HiPPO) used carefully designed $A$ matrices (HiPPO matrices) to enable long-term memory in the state. But $A, B, C$ are fixed for every input — no ability to "selectively attend" to different positions. Performs worse than Transformer.

Mamba's Selectivity

Mamba's (2023, Albert Gu) key innovation: making $B, C, \Delta$ (step size) depend on the input $\mathbf{x}_t$. The "memory dynamics" at each position differ — the model can selectively "remember" or "forget."

$B_t = \text{Linear}_B(\mathbf{x}_t), \quad C_t = \text{Linear}_C(\mathbf{x}_t), \quad \Delta_t = \text{softplus}(\text{Linear}_\Delta(\mathbf{x}_t))$

This gives the model "dynamic content addressing" capability — similar selectivity to attention, but using $O(L)$ state updates rather than $O(L^2)$ full comparisons.

Hardware-Aware Scanning

Selective SSM has an engineering challenge: input-dependent $B_t, C_t$ means traditional "FFT-accelerated convolution" doesn't work. Mamba uses a "hardware-aware parallel scan" algorithm — based on prefix-sum ideas, maintaining parallelism on GPUs.

Training: forward pass can use parallel scan, $O(L)$ FLOPs but $O(\log L)$ wall-clock time.

Inference: pure recurrence, each step $O(1)$ — fixed state size. No KV Cache! Memory usage doesn't grow with sequence length.

Hybrid Architectures

Early work like Jamba (AI21) and Zamba explored "Transformer + Mamba hybrid" — most layers use Mamba (fast), a few layers use attention (precise recall). By 2025, this direction became one of the mainstream approaches:

  • IBM Granite 4: Mamba-Attention hybrid + MoE; enterprise production use

  • Qwen3.5 full lineup: all adopt a Gated Delta Networks + MoE hybrid architecture

This trend shows: Mamba is not "replacing Transformer" but serving as a complementary layer for long-context and low-memory scenarios, combining with attention to form a "better architecture."

There's also a path of continuing innovation within pure Transformer — DeepSeek V3.2's DSA optimizes within attention itself, achieving similar effects without taking the Mamba route. Both technical paths run in parallel through 2025-2026.

PART FIVE

4.5 Tokenizer and Embedding

The first gate for text entering the model — tokenizer details affect every aspect of LLM capability.

4.5.1The BPE Family

Position: From BPE to WordPiece to SentencePiece — the three main lines of modern tokenizers.

Why Not Character-Level, Why Not Word-Level

Two extremes:

Character-level: small vocabulary (128 ASCII characters in English), one token per character. Problem: long sequences — a 5-letter word becomes 5 tokens, causing $O(L^2)$ explosion in attention.

Word-level: natural vocabulary (one token per word). Problem: vocabulary explosion (millions of words in English), OOV (out-of-vocabulary words not seen during training), unable to handle spelling variations. Chinese "word" boundaries are fuzzy, making it more problematic.

Subword is the compromise: common words are a single token; rare words are split into meaningful subwords. "unbelievable" → ["un", "believ", "able"].

BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding)

Originated as a data compression algorithm in 1994; borrowed for NMT tokenization in 2015.

Training:

  1. Initialize vocabulary as all characters
  2. Count the frequency of adjacent token pairs in the corpus
  3. Merge the most frequent pair and add it to the vocabulary
  4. Repeat steps 2-3 until the vocabulary reaches the target size (e.g., 50K)
from collections import Counter

def train_bpe(corpus, vocab_size):
    words = Counter()
    for line in corpus:
        for w in line.split():
            words[' '.join(list(w)) + ' </w>'] += 1
    vocab = set()
    for w in words: vocab.update(w.split())
    while len(vocab) < vocab_size:
        pairs = Counter()
        for w, freq in words.items():
            toks = w.split()
            for i in range(len(toks) - 1):
                pairs[(toks[i], toks[i+1])] += freq
        if not pairs: break
        best = pairs.most_common(1)[0][0]
        merged = ''.join(best)
        new_words = {}
        for w, freq in words.items():
            new_w = w.replace(' '.join(best), merged)
            new_words[new_w] = freq
        words = new_words
        vocab.add(merged)
    return vocab

Inference: apply the trained merge rules recursively to new text to get the token sequence.

WordPiece

Google's variant used in BERT. Difference: instead of merging "the most frequent pair," merge "the pair that maximizes language model likelihood." Similar results but theoretically cleaner.

SentencePiece

Open-sourced by Google in 2018. Addresses several pain points:

  1. No pre-tokenization: trains directly on raw text, doesn't rely on the concept of "words" — friendly to languages without spaces like Chinese, Japanese, Korean
  2. Reversible: tokenization can perfectly restore the original string (including spaces) — via a special ▁ symbol marking word beginnings
  3. Unigram algorithm: a different merging strategy from BPE; starts with a large vocabulary and prunes

LLaMA, T5, mT5, and ChatGLM all use SentencePiece.

BBPE: Byte-level BPE

The variant used by GPT-2. First convert text to UTF-8 byte sequences (256 possible values), then apply BPE on the byte sequence. Benefit: zero OOV — any Unicode character can be represented (at least as a byte sequence), no [UNK] token needed. GPT-3/4, LLaMA, Qwen, DeepSeek all use BBPE. It is the de facto standard.

4.5.3Embedding Models

Position: From Word2Vec to BGE — the model family specialized for text similarity, a key component of RAG.

From Word Embeddings to Sentence Embeddings

Early Word2Vec and GloVe were "word embeddings" — one fixed vector per word. But "bank" means different things in "river bank" and "bank account"; fixed vectors can't handle this.

The BERT era brought "contextual embeddings" — the same word has different vectors in different contexts. But BERT output is token-level; how do you get a representation for a whole sentence?

  • CLS token: during BERT pretraining, [CLS] represents the whole sentence, but in practice this representation performs poorly for similarity tasks
  • Mean pooling: average representations over all tokens; simple but loses precision
  • Sentence-BERT: fine-tune specifically with a dual-encoder structure to make "similar sentences have similar representations" — qualitative improvement for similarity tasks

Training Methods

Modern embedding training is multi-stage: weakly supervised pretraining (contrastive learning from web text pairs); supervised fine-tuning (refining with labeled similar pairs); hard negative mining (finding pairs that look similar but aren't).

Contrastive Learning Loss

The core of embedding model training is contrastive loss; the typical InfoNCE loss:

$\mathcal{L} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(q, p^+) / \tau)}{\exp(\text{sim}(q, p^+) / \tau) + \sum_{p^-} \exp(\text{sim}(q, p^-) / \tau)}$

$q, p^+$ are positive pairs; $p^-$ are negative samples; $\tau$ is the temperature. Goal: make $q$ more similar to $p^+$ and farther from all $p^-$.

Matryoshka Embedding

A novel idea: train a 1024-dimensional embedding, but also make the first 64, first 256, and first 512 dimensions individually valid. Users can truncate as needed — take the full vector for quality, take the first N dimensions for speed.

OpenAI text-embedding-3 and some open-source models (like Matryoshka-BGE) support this feature. Flexible trade-off between storage and performance.

Embedding Quality Evaluation

MTEB (Massive Text Embedding Benchmark) is the industry standard — 56 tasks evaluating embedding models. Covers classification, clustering, retrieval, STS, and reranking. The leaderboard changes every few weeks, but NV-Embed, BGE-M3, and Qwen3-Embedding are in the first tier. Real-world selection requires testing on your specific use case.

PART SIX

4.6 Mixture of Experts

Sparse activation makes "trillion-parameter models" computationally feasible — nearly all the largest open-source models use MoE.

4.6.1MoE Fundamentals

Position: Why MoE, how MoE — core mathematics and basic architecture.

Motivation

Transformer parameters are mainly in FFN (computed in 4.2.1 as 2/3 of the total). FFN applies the same processing to every token — but different tokens may need different processing (nouns need different features than verbs). MoE's idea: replace a single FFN with multiple "expert" FFNs, each token activating only 1-2 experts. Effect: total parameters multiply, but compute barely increases.

Structure

A MoE layer:

  1. Router: compute a score for each expert given input $\mathbf{x}$: $g_i = \text{softmax}(W_{router} \mathbf{x})_i$
  2. Top-K selection: select the K highest-scoring experts (typically K=1 or 2)
  3. Routing: send $\mathbf{x}$ only to selected experts; other experts don't compute
  4. Weighted combination: $\mathbf{y} = \sum_{i \in \text{top-k}} g_i \cdot \text{Expert}_i(\mathbf{x})$

Compute-Parameter Trade-off

Given $N$ total experts, each with the same parameters as the original FFN, activating K experts:

  • Total parameters: approximately $N \times$ original FFN parameters + attention layer parameters
  • Active parameters (actually participating in forward pass): $K \times$ original FFN + attention
  • Compute: proportional to active parameters

Mixtral 8x7B: 8 experts each ≈ 7B, activating 2. Total parameters 47B (not 56B, because attention is shared); active ≈ 13B — inference like a 13B model, capability close to a 47B dense model. This is MoE's core value proposition.

Routing Function

class Router(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, n_experts, top_k=2):
        super().__init__()
        self.W = nn.Linear(d_model, n_experts, bias=False)
        self.top_k = top_k

    def forward(self, x):
        logits = self.W(x)
        top_k_logits, top_k_idx = logits.topk(self.top_k, dim=-1)
        top_k_gates = top_k_logits.softmax(dim=-1)
        return top_k_idx, top_k_gates

Load Balancing

The biggest problem: if training is improper, routing degenerates — always sending traffic to a few experts, starving the others.

Solution: load balancing loss. Define $f_i$ = fraction of tokens routed to expert $i$; $P_i$ = average gate value for expert $i$ over all tokens. Add an auxiliary loss:

$\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = N \sum_i f_i P_i$

Ideally $f_i = P_i = 1/N$, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{aux}} = 1$. If an expert is overloaded, both $f_i$ and $P_i$ are large, increasing the loss and pushing gradients to rebalance routing.

Sparse vs Dense

Almost all new MoE models choose the fine-grained expert architecture — many small experts (DeepSeek-V3 has 256, each small). Better than "8 large experts": more specialized experts, more diverse combinations, more natural load balancing.

Shared Experts

DeepSeek-V2/V3 uses "shared experts + routed experts": 1-2 shared experts that all tokens pass through, with the other N-2 selected by routing. Intuition: some knowledge (grammar, basic logic) is needed by all tokens and doesn't need to be routed each time.

4.6.2MoE Engineering Practice

Position: MoE looks good on paper, is hard in engineering — training, deployment, communication, and load balancing all need to be solved.

Training Challenges

Instability: routing is discrete (top-k selection), making gradients hard to propagate. Early training sees routing thrash around with no expert specialization — requires very long warmup to stabilize.

Expert parallelism: 8 experts can be placed on 8 GPUs, but this requires cross-device communication at every layer (tokens distributed by routing, then collected after computation). All-to-all communication becomes a bottleneck.

Dynamic shape: different experts receive different numbers of tokens — a small expert might receive only a few, a large one might receive tens of thousands. GPUs prefer regular batch shapes; dynamic shapes make kernel optimization difficult.

Expert Parallelism

MoE's distributed strategy has its own name: Expert Parallelism (EP). Distribute N experts evenly across P GPUs, N/P experts per GPU.

Forward flow: each GPU's tokens go through attention (normal TP/DP); Router determines which expert each token needs; All-to-all: each GPU sends tokens to the GPU holding the corresponding expert; each GPU's active experts process the received tokens; All-to-all: send results back to the GPU where the tokens originated.

Two all-to-all communications with large data volume, but acceptable under high-speed interconnects (NVLink + IB). DeepSpeed-MoE and Megatron-LM both implement this.

Token Dropping

Under load imbalance, an expert may receive more tokens than its capacity (capacity factor). Handling options: Drop (tokens exceeding capacity skip this layer — simple but loses information); Capacity Bias (add capacity penalty during training to encourage uniform routing).

Real large models set capacity factor to 1.25 (allowing 25% overflow), with minimal dropping.

MoE Inference Deployment

Inference is trickier than training:

Memory usage: all experts must be loaded (since routing destination is unknown in advance). Mixtral 8x7B inference requires 80GB+ — not the "feels like 13B" experience.

Batching efficiency: under small batch sizes, most experts idle (no tokens routed to them), GPU utilization is extremely low. MoE inference performance only shines at large batch sizes.

Expert placement: for single-machine multi-GPU inference, how to assign experts? Heuristic: place high-frequency experts on GPUs with closer communication; cold experts go farther or offload to CPU. vLLM, SGLang, and TGI all have specialized optimizations for MoE inference.

PART SEVEN

4.7 Distributed Training

Models above the tens-of-billions scale don't fit on a single GPU — must span across GPUs and machines.

4.7.1Data Parallelism (DDP)

Position: The simplest distributed strategy — complete model on each GPU, different batch shards.

Basic Idea

$N$ GPUs, each holding a complete model replica. A large batch $B$ split into $N$ parts, $B/N$ per GPU. Each GPU computes its own forward loss and backward gradients. Gradients are AllReduced to aggregate into the full-batch average gradient; each GPU updates parameters with this gradient — parameters stay synchronized.

AllReduce

The core communication primitive of DDP. Given one tensor (gradient) per GPU, compute the sum (or average) of all GPUs' tensors, with each GPU ending up with the same result.

Multiple implementations exist; the most popular is Ring AllReduce: split each GPU's tensor into $N$ chunks; first phase sends rings, each round each GPU adds one chunk to the next, after $N-1$ rounds each GPU has the complete sum of one chunk; second phase broadcasts each GPU's "completed chunk" around the ring, after $N-1$ more rounds all GPUs have all complete chunks.

Communication volume: each GPU sends and receives $2(N-1)/N \cdot S$ ($S$ = tensor size), nearly independent of $N$ — excellent scalability. NCCL is the de facto standard implementation on GPUs.

PyTorch DDP

import torch
import torch.distributed as dist
from torch.nn.parallel import DistributedDataParallel as DDP

dist.init_process_group(backend='nccl')
local_rank = int(os.environ['LOCAL_RANK'])
torch.cuda.set_device(local_rank)

model = MyModel().cuda()
model = DDP(model, device_ids=[local_rank])

from torch.utils.data.distributed import DistributedSampler
sampler = DistributedSampler(dataset)
loader = DataLoader(dataset, sampler=sampler, batch_size=per_rank_bs)

for batch in loader:
    loss = model(batch).mean()
    loss.backward()     # DDP automatically all-reduces gradients
    optimizer.step()

Gradient Accumulation

Sometimes a large batch is desired but a single GPU can't hold the full batch. Gradient accumulation: run multiple forward+backward passes without calling step, accumulating until enough, then step. Combined with DDP to reduce gradient sync frequency (only one all-reduce just before step).

DDP Limitations

DDP's primary limitation: each GPU must fit the complete model + gradients + optimizer state.

Computing single-GPU requirements for a 7B FP16 model: parameters 14GB; gradients 14GB; Adam state (m, v, FP32) 56GB; activations ~10GB. Total ~100GB; A100 80G isn't enough, nor is H100 80G. So training above 7B requires sharding — enter ZeRO.

4.7.2ZeRO Three Stages

Position: DeepSpeed's flagship technology — sharding optimizer state, gradients, and parameters to achieve linear memory reduction.

Core Insight

In DDP, each GPU stores complete parameters, gradients, and optimizer state — but each GPU actually only needs to process its own portion of parameter updates; the state for other parameters is redundant. ZeRO (Zero Redundancy Optimizer, Microsoft 2020) says: shard these redundancies across different GPUs, fetching them via communication when needed.

Three Stages

ZeRO's three stages progressively shard more:

StageWhat's ShardedCommunication OverheadMemory Savings (N GPUs)
ZeRO-1Optimizer stateSame as DDPOptimizer /N
ZeRO-2+ GradientsSlightly moreOptimizer and gradients /N
ZeRO-3+ ParametersNoticeably moreEverything /N

ZeRO-1

Simplest. Each GPU still stores complete parameters and gradients, but only $1/N$ of optimizer state (only responsible for updating $1/N$ of parameters).

Update flow: all GPUs forward; all GPUs backward, getting complete gradients (AllReduce as in DDP); each GPU only updates its responsible $1/N$ parameters (using its stored optimizer state); AllGather updated parameter shards to synchronize globally.

Adam state is the biggest component (8 bytes/parameter); ZeRO-1 already saves most of it.

ZeRO-2

Gradients are also not stored in full. During backward, ReduceScatter (reduce and scatter simultaneously), so each GPU ends up with only the gradients for its responsible $1/N$ parameters. Communication volume similar to ZeRO-1 (just different operation names). Further memory savings: no more full gradients.

ZeRO-3

Parameters are also sharded. Each GPU permanently holds only $1/N$ of parameters. Computing a layer during forward: AllGather that layer's complete parameters (all GPUs contribute their shard); immediately release after the layer completes (keep only your own shard); AllGather again for the next layer. Backward is the same — temporarily gather when needed, discard after use.

Memory reduced to the extreme: 7B model on 8 GPUs requires only ~12-15GB per GPU. Trade-off: forward and backward both require an extra AllGather round, noticeably increasing communication overhead.

FSDP

PyTorch's official ZeRO-3 implementation is called FSDP (Fully Sharded Data Parallel). Simpler interface than DeepSpeed, better integration with the PyTorch ecosystem.

from torch.distributed.fsdp import FullyShardedDataParallel as FSDP
from torch.distributed.fsdp import MixedPrecision

mp = MixedPrecision(
    param_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    reduce_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
    buffer_dtype=torch.bfloat16,
)
model = FSDP(
    model,
    mixed_precision=mp,
    sharding_strategy=ShardingStrategy.FULL_SHARD,  # ZeRO-3
    auto_wrap_policy=size_based_auto_wrap_policy,
    device_id=local_rank,
)

ZeRO-Offload and Infinity

More extreme ZeRO variants:

  • ZeRO-Offload: offload optimizer state and gradients to CPU memory, pulling back when updates are needed. Can train 10B+ models on a single GPU, but 2-3× slower.
  • ZeRO-Infinity: further offload to NVMe SSD. Theoretically enables trillion-parameter training on a single machine; in practice extremely slow. Suitable for research, not production.

When to Use ZeRO

Based on parameter count: < 1B: DDP suffices; 1B-10B: ZeRO-2 for balance; 10B-100B: ZeRO-3 or FSDP; 100B+: ZeRO-3 + tensor parallelism + pipeline parallelism.

4.7.3Tensor Parallelism and Pipeline Parallelism

Position: Splitting individual layer computations (TP), splitting the layer sequence (PP) — distributing "large models" across multiple GPUs.

Tensor Parallelism (TP)

Split the matrix multiplication within a single layer. Most typical: Linear layer $\mathbf{Y} = \mathbf{X} W$.

Column-parallel: split $W$ by columns into $[W_1, W_2, \ldots]$, each GPU holds a portion. After computation, each GPU has different columns of $\mathbf{Y}$; concatenate or AllGather to merge.

Row-parallel: split $W$ by rows; input $\mathbf{X}$ is also split by columns correspondingly. Each GPU computes a partial result; AllReduce to sum.

Megatron-LM's attention and FFN TP combination: in Attention, $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ are column-parallel (each GPU computes partial heads), $W_O$ is row-parallel — no communication inside attention, one AllReduce at the exit; in FFN, $W_1$ is column-parallel, $W_2$ is row-parallel — no communication inside FFN, one AllReduce at the exit.

This requires only 2 AllReduces per layer. Communication is a bottleneck but manageable.

TP constraint: requires high-speed interconnect. TP works within a GPU group connected by NVLink (typically 8 GPUs); InfiniBand bandwidth across nodes isn't sufficient to support TP.

Pipeline Parallelism (PP)

Split an $L$-layer model into $P$ segments, one segment per GPU. A batch of data flows sequentially across GPUs.

The naive implementation has a "bubble" problem — most GPUs idle most of the time. Solution: split the batch into micro-batches and pipeline execution (1F1B schedule).

GPipe (2018): split batch into $M$ micro-batches, perform $M$ sequential forwards then $M$ backwards. Bubble fraction $(P-1)/(P+M-1)$; small when $M$ is large.

PipeDream / 1F1B: interleave forward and backward passes; fewer bubbles.

Interleaved 1F1B (Megatron): each GPU holds multiple non-contiguous segments; bubbles further reduced.

4.7.43D Parallelism and Training Stack

Position: Combining DP + TP + PP — the standard approach for 100B+ models. Also covering Megatron/DeepSpeed/FSDP.

3D Parallelism

Real large-model training uses all three dimensions together:

  • TP: within node (NVLink 8-GPU group), splits single layers
  • PP: cross-node (IB connected), splits layer sequence
  • DP: the "remaining" dimension beyond the above two, splits data

Example with 512 GPUs (64 nodes × 8 GPUs): TP=8 (within node), PP=8 (8 groups of nodes), DP=8 (data parallelism dimension). $8 \times 8 \times 8 = 512$.

Model state per GPU: holds $\frac{1}{8}$ of single-layer parameters (TP); holds $\frac{1}{8}$ of layers (PP); sees $\frac{1}{8}$ of the batch (DP). Actual memory pressure per GPU ≈ model / 64, manageable.

Adding ZeRO

Apply ZeRO on the DP dimension to further save memory: ZeRO-1 (optimizer state sharded along DP dimension); FSDP (parameters, gradients, optimizer all sharded along DP).

GPT-4-scale training is rumored to use TP=8 + PP=16 + DP=64 + ZeRO-1, across 8192 H100s total.

Activation Recomputation

Backward pass needs intermediate activations from the forward pass. Storing them all consumes significant memory. Gradient Checkpointing: only keep activations at a few "checkpoint" positions; recompute from the nearest checkpoint during backward. Typically only keep each Transformer block's input; recompute one forward per block during backward — 30% extra compute in exchange for 3-5× reduction in activation memory. Essential for training 100B+ models.

PART EIGHT

4.8 Inference Engines and Scheduling

Training is just the beginning — inference optimization is the key to whether LLMs can be practically deployed.

4.8.1Inference Workload Characteristics

Position: Prefill vs Decode — LLM inference's two phases have fundamentally different bottlenecks.

Two-Phase Model

LLM inference has two phases:

Prefill: process the entire input prompt (e.g., 1000 tokens); one forward pass computes the KV Cache for all tokens and predicts the next token.

Decode: based on the existing KV Cache, generate one new token per step — each forward pass processes only 1 new token but must read all historical KV Cache to perform attention.

A typical conversation: 100-token prompt + 500-token response = 1 prefill (100 tokens) + 500 decode steps.

Compute Characteristic Differences

Per-forward processingEntire prompt (hundreds to thousands of tokens)1 token

Batching benefitSmall (prompt is already large)Large (combining multiple requests)

Decode's bottleneck is memory bandwidth. Generating each token requires reading all layers' KV Cache from HBM for attention. Model weights also need a full pass. Compute is minimal but data movement is extensive — GPU compute utilization drops to 5-10%. This characteristic drives the entire inference optimization field.

Key Metrics

  • TTFT (Time To First Token): time from request to first token returned. Determined by prefill. User-perceived latency.
  • TPOT (Time Per Output Token): average time between tokens. Determined by decode. Determines generation speed.
  • Throughput: system-wide tokens/s — most important for service providers.
  • Concurrency: how many requests can be served simultaneously.

Latency and throughput trade off. Large batches increase throughput but lengthen TPOT — how to schedule is the core problem of inference engines.

Hardware Bottleneck Analysis

H100 with LLaMA-3-70B FP16 decode: model weights 140GB; HBM bandwidth 3 TB/s; theoretical limit 3000 / 140 ≈ 21 tokens/s (single request).

Measured decode 25-40 tokens/s (vLLM data) — essentially approaching the bandwidth ceiling. Improvements only possible via: compressing weights (quantization), compressing KV Cache, or batching multiple requests (sharing bandwidth across requests).

4.8.2KV Cache

Position: The soul of LLM inference — cutting $O(L^2)$ complexity to $O(L)$, but introducing memory pressure.

Why It's Needed

During decode, generating the $t$-th token requires only the new token's $\mathbf{q}_t$ for attention, but needs all prior positions' K, V . If $\mathbf{k}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{k}_{t-1}$ were recomputed each time, that's $O(t)$ extra computation per step — total $O(t^2)$. KV Cache caches all prior K, V — each new token only computes its own $\mathbf{k}_t, \mathbf{v}_t$ and appends to the cache. Total computation $O(t)$.

Memory Footprint

LLaMA-3-70B (80 layers, 64 heads, d_head=128, GQA=8):

KV per token per layer: $2 \times 8 \times 128 \times 2 = 4$ KB (FP16)

KV per token total: $80 \times 4$ KB $= 320$ KB

A 10K token conversation: 3.2 GB per sample

A single H100 80G can serve only ~20 such conversations simultaneously — memory is the concurrency ceiling.

Problems with Naive KV Cache

Early inference engines (original Transformers, early TGI) allocated each sample's KV Cache as contiguous, maximum-length pre-reserved space.

Problems: wasted memory (reserving max_length=4096 but only using 500, 87.5% wasted); internal fragmentation (different request lengths, poor space utilization); external fragmentation (new requests need large contiguous blocks; small blocks freed by short requests don't suffice); low concurrency ceiling. This is what PagedAttention was designed to solve.

4.8.3PagedAttention and vLLM

Orientation: OS virtual memory applied to KV Cache — vLLM's core innovation.

Core Idea

PagedAttention's (UC Berkeley 2023) inspiration comes directly from OS virtual memory: physical memory is divided into fixed-size pages; logical addresses are mapped to physical pages via a page table.

Applied to KV Cache: partition GPU memory into fixed-size blocks (e.g., each block holds KV for 16 tokens); each request maintains a block table mapping its logical token positions → physical blocks; blocks are allocated on demand, not pre-reserved; the Attention kernel looks up actual positions via the block table.

Results

  • - Memory utilization: from ~40% to ~96% (official data)
  • - Concurrency: 4-10× more concurrent requests on the same 80G memory
  • - Throughput: 2-4× improvement over prior approaches

Attention Kernel Modification

Standard attention kernels assume K, V are in contiguous memory. PagedAttention's kernel must look up K, V positions via the block table:

// simplified pseudocode
for each query q_i:
    output = 0; max_score = -inf; sum_exp = 0
    for each logical token j in query's block_table:
        block_id  = block_table[j / BLOCK_SIZE]
        inner_idx = j % BLOCK_SIZE
        k_j = K_pool[block_id * BLOCK_SIZE + inner_idx]
        v_j = V_pool[block_id * BLOCK_SIZE + inner_idx]
        score = q_i @ k_j / sqrt(d)
        // online softmax update
        new_max = max(max_score, score)
        sum_exp = sum_exp * exp(max_score - new_max) + exp(score - new_max)
        output  = output  * exp(max_score - new_max) + exp(score - new_max) * v_j
        max_score = new_max

The extra indirection has minor overhead, far outweighed by the memory pressure saved.

Copy-on-Write and Sharing

PagedAttention also supports KV Cache sharing — similar to OS fork+COW. Scenario: beam search or parallel sampling where multiple candidates share the same prefix. Naive approach: one KV Cache per candidate; PagedAttention lets multiple sequences' block tables point to the same physical block, copying only on write.

Also applies to Prefix Caching: multiple requests share the same system prompt; the prompt portion's KV Cache can be shared — computed only once. QPS for high-concurrency LLM services increases dramatically.

vLLM and Others

The reference implementation of PagedAttention. vLLM has grown from a research project to the de facto standard open-source inference engine: PagedAttention + continuous batching + speculative decoding; broad HuggingFace model compatibility; TP and multi-GPU support; OpenAI-compatible API; active community with fast updates.

Not the only option. SGLang has its own RadixAttention (prefix sharing organized as a radix tree), faster for complex workflows (multi-turn, multi-candidate). TGI (HuggingFace's Rust implementation) is stable. TensorRT-LLM is extremely optimized on NVIDIA platforms but has a closed ecosystem.

4.8.4Continuous Batching

Position: The evolution of dynamic batching — efficiently parallelizing requests of different lengths rather than waiting for a full batch.

Traditional Static Batching

Early approach: a batch of requests starts and ends together, padded to the longest length.

Problems: must wait for a full batch before starting — high latency; when request lengths vary greatly, short requests idle waiting for long ones — low throughput; finished requests wait for others in the same batch, GPU idles.

Continuous Batching

The approach popularized by Orca (2022) and vLLM (2023). Core: schedule at the iteration (each decode step) granularity, not the whole request.

Each decode step: check current active requests, form a batch for one forward; update each request's output; finished requests return immediately and free resources; new requests join the batch in the next iteration.

GPU utilization rises from 40% to 80%+.

Prefill/Decode Scheduling

A detail: prefill and decode have very different compute characteristics, so mixing them in scheduling isn't always most efficient. Two styles:

Iteration-level mixing (early vLLM): each iteration may have both prefill and decode requests. Prefill is compute-bound, decode is memory-bound; mixing complements each other — but iteration time is prolonged by prefill, affecting decode TPOT.

Chunked Prefill (supported by vLLM, SGLang): split long-prompt prefill into chunks; each iteration mixes a small amount of prefill with decode — balancing the two.

Prefill-Decode separation (SplitWise, DistServe): prefill and decode run on different GPUs/machines — prefill machines are compute-intensive, decode machines are bandwidth-intensive. KV Cache transferred cross-node. Suitable for ultra-large scale.

Preemption and Swap-Out

What to do when GPU memory is full? Preempt some requests — swap their KV Cache to CPU (or simply discard and recompute).

Policies: First-Come-First-Served (FCFS), priority (premium users first), or swap fairness. vLLM implements these mechanisms; robust in production environments.

4.8.5Speculative Decoding

Position: A small model guesses, a large model verifies — 2-3× inference speedup with no quality loss.

Basic Idea

The primary bottleneck of decode is that each forward produces only 1 token. Can multiple tokens be produced at once? Standard methods can't — each token depends on the previous one.

Speculative Decoding (independently proposed by Google and DeepMind): use a small model (draft) to quickly generate K candidate tokens; use a large model (target) in one forward pass to verify whether these K tokens conform to its distribution; accept conforming tokens (sequentially from the front), reject from the first non-conforming token onward.

Key insight: the large model's single forward computed logits for K positions, completing "verification + next token generation" in one step.

Why the Distribution Is Unchanged

Speculative decoding's mathematical guarantee: the output distribution is identical to using the large model directly (statistically). Derivation:

Let the small model distribution be $q(x)$, the large model distribution be $p(x)$. The acceptance probability for candidate token $x$:

$\min\left(1, \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}\right)$

On rejection, sample from the adjusted distribution:

$p'(x) = \frac{\max(0, p(x) - q(x))}{\sum_{x'} \max(0, p(x') - q(x'))}$

This rejection sampling mechanism guarantees the final sampling distribution is exactly $p(x)$.

Acceptance Rate

Efficiency depends on the "alignment" between the small and large models. Higher alignment → higher acceptance rate → greater speedup.

Typical pairing: LLaMA-3-70B + LLaMA-3-8B: acceptance rate ~70%, speedup 2-3×; same-family small/large model pairings work best — same family means similar training distributions.

Medusa and EAGLE

Variants: instead of an independent small model, add a few "heads" to the target model to directly predict multiple future tokens.

Medusa (2024): add K extra heads on top of the original model, each predicting one position of the next K tokens. Adding a few hundred M parameters; K candidates generated in parallel during inference.

EAGLE / EAGLE-2: more advanced — not "multi-head prediction" but a lightweight autoregressive sub-network that generates candidates from the main model's hidden states. Acceptance rate can reach 80%+, speedup 3×+.

Engineering Trade-offs

Speculative decoding trade-offs: extra memory (small model or extra heads); complex implementation with interaction pitfalls with batching/KV Cache; large benefit under small batch; diminishing benefit under large batch (memory-bound pressure reduced).

vLLM, TGI, and TensorRT-LLM all support speculative decoding. Significant benefit for high-QPS online serving; small benefit for internal tooling services — may not be worth the complexity.

4.9 Inference Compression

Quantization is not an "add-on" to inference speedup; it is the prerequisite for running open-source large models at all.

4.9.1AWQ / GPTQ Deep Dive

Position: The two mainstream LLM quantization methods — mathematical principles and engineering differences.

LLM Quantization Specifics

Chapter 3 Section 4.7.2 covered general quantization principles. LLMs are special: enormous weight count (70B × 2B = 140GB); INT4 compression brings this to 35GB — fits on a single A100/H100; activations have extreme outliers — some dimensions have very large values that suffer severe loss with standard quantization; high precision requirements — a single wrong token can derail an entire response.

GPTQ

GPTQ (2022) uses second-order information for column-by-column quantization. Core observation: quantization error introduced by one column of weights can be compensated by adjusting the remaining columns.

Mathematically, solving:

$\arg\min_{\hat{W}} \| \mathbf{X}(W - \hat{W}) \|^2$

Use a small amount of calibration data to approximate the Hessian; quantize column by column: for each column $j$, find the nearest quantized value $\hat{w}_j$; compute error $\delta = w_j - \hat{w}_j$; "absorb" the error into remaining unquantized columns to minimize total error; continue to the next column.

Algorithm complexity $O(d^3)$ ($d$ = layer width); quantizing one layer takes a few minutes. INT4 quantization achieves 95%+ of FP16 precision.

AWQ

AWQ (Activation-aware Weight Quantization, MIT 2023) observed: not all weights are equally important — weights corresponding to large activation magnitudes are more important.

Traditional quantization treats all weights equally; quantization error for "important weights" is amplified (large activation × quantization error = large loss). AWQ's fix: pre-scale important channels to reduce their relative quantization error.

Specifically, for each column of the weight matrix (corresponding to one activation channel):

$W' = W \cdot s, \quad \hat{W} = \text{Quantize}(W')$

During inference, multiply activations by $s$: $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{x} / s$. Mathematically equivalent but quantization error distribution changes — important weights have smaller error; unimportant weights have larger error (doesn't matter).

How to choose $s$? Measure activation magnitudes using calibration data; important channels use $s > 1$ (weight amplified; smaller relative quantization error); unimportant channels use $s \le 1$.

In practice AWQ is more commonly chosen — simpler engineering, same inference speed, negligible precision difference.

GGUF and llama.cpp

The quantization format used by llama.cpp is GGUF, with its own quantization schemes (Q4_K_M, Q5_K_S, Q8_0, etc.). Similar to AWQ/GPTQ but optimized to the extreme on both CPU and GPU. First choice for edge deployment (personal computers, phones).

Ultra-Low Bit

The 2024 frontier is INT2/INT1. BitNet (Microsoft) trains with ternary (-1, 0, +1) weights but requires training from scratch.

More practical are QuIP and AQLM — first apply an orthogonal transform to "smooth" weights, then quantize to 2-3 bits; precision approaches INT4.

General production deployment remains INT4 mainstream — precision starts degrading noticeably below INT3.

4.9.2KV Cache Quantization and FP8

Position: Beyond weights — KV Cache and activations also need compression to further free memory.

KV Cache Quantization

INT4 weight quantization is now standard, but KV Cache remains FP16 — at long contexts it can be larger than the model weights.

INT8 KV Cache: simple and effective. K, V per token quantized by channel. Memory halved; precision almost unchanged. Supported by vLLM and TensorRT-LLM.

INT4 KV Cache: memory halved again, but precision loss starts to become noticeable. Requires careful group quantization (one scale per 64 or 128 tokens) to maintain quality.

KIVI (2024): Key quantized by channel, Value quantized by token — because Key and Value have different distribution characteristics. Can maintain precision at INT2.

KV Cache quantization offers maximum benefit in long-context scenarios. A 100K token conversation's KV Cache drops from 30GB to 7.5GB (INT4), completely changing deployment possibilities.

FP8 and Hopper

NVIDIA H100 Hopper architecture natively supports FP8 — two formats:

  • E4M3: 4-bit exponent, 3-bit mantissa. Smaller dynamic range, relatively higher precision. Stores weights and activations.
  • E5M2: 5-bit exponent, 2-bit mantissa. Larger dynamic range, lower precision. Stores gradients and other quantities where outliers must be preserved.

FP8 compute doubles compared to FP16 on H100 Tensor Cores. Importantly, FP8 can also be used for training — unlike INT8 which is inference-only.

Next generation: NVIDIA Blackwell. In 2024, NVIDIA released the Blackwell architecture (B200/GB200), with single-chip FP4 compute reaching 20 PFLOPS and 192GB HBM3e — the next-generation flagship training/inference chip after the H100. Blackwell supports FP4 precision natively, further reducing inference cost; NVLink 5 provides 1.8 TB/s inter-chip bandwidth. From 2025, major cloud providers began deploying GB200 clusters, which have become the new standard for large-scale LLM training.

FP8 Training

DeepSeek-V3, LLaMA-3, and Qwen 2.5 have all tried FP8 training. Key technical points: forward activations use FP8; backward gradients use FP8; weight master copies remain FP32 (for optimizer updates); per-tensor or per-token scaling prevents outlier overflow; some sensitive operations (normalization, softmax) retain FP16/FP32.

DeepSeek-V3 measured FP8 as 1.5-2× faster than BF16 with almost no precision loss. One of the major 2024 training advances.

FP8 Inference

FP8's compute advantage in inference + smaller memory footprint than FP16. But toolchain maturity currently lags INT4 — the main challenge is dynamic range: INT4 relies on group quantize calibration; FP8's per-tensor scale requires more precision.

TensorRT-LLM has the best FP8 inference support (being NVIDIA's product). vLLM and SGLang are following suit.

KV Cache + Weight Dual Quantization

Extreme compression route: INT4 weights + INT4 KV Cache. LLaMA-3-70B on a single H100 (80G):

  • - Weights: 140GB FP16 → 35GB INT4
  • - KV Cache: 2.5GB per sample at 8K context → 0.6GB INT4
  • - Concurrency: from under 10 to 50+

This is the key to serving open-source 70B models at scale on a single H100.

PART TEN

4.10 RAG

Giving LLMs access to what they don't know — enterprise knowledge, real-time data, and private documents all flow through this gateway.

4.10.1Basic RAG Pipeline

Orientation: the complete naive RAG workflow — design decisions at every step.

Why RAG?

An LLM's parameters encode knowledge from training time. Three fundamental problems:

  1. Staleness: training data has a cutoff; the model knows nothing after that point
  2. Hallucination: when the model doesn't know, it fabricates
  3. Private data: enterprise-internal documents were never seen during training

Solution A is fine-tuning — expensive, and must be repeated every time new knowledge arrives. Solution B is RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation): at query time, retrieve relevant documents from a knowledge base, splice them into the prompt, and let the model answer based on the retrieved context.

Naive RAG Pipeline

flowchart LR subgraph Offline DOC[Documents] --> CHUNK[Chunking] CHUNK --> EMB[Embedding] EMB --> VDB[Vector DB] end subgraph Online Q[Query] --> QE[Query Embedding] QE --> SEARCH[ANN Search] VDB --> SEARCH SEARCH --> CTX[Retrieved Chunks] CTX --> PROMPT[Prompt Construction] Q --> PROMPT PROMPT --> LLM[LLM] LLM --> A[Answer] end

Chunking

Documents are split into small chunks, each embedded independently. Why? A vector must represent the full chunk content — chunks that are too large can't be captured in a single vector; the LLM's context is limited, so overly large chunks leave room for fewer of them; and retrieval granularity matters — user queries typically match a specific passage, not an entire document.

Splitting strategies:

  1. Fixed size: one chunk per 500 tokens, the simplest approach. Drawback: sentences get cut mid-way.
  2. Recursive splitting: prefer paragraph boundaries, fall back to sentence boundaries, then fixed size. This is the default in LangChain's RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter.
  3. Semantic splitting: split at points where consecutive sentence similarity drops sharply.
  4. Structure-aware: split Markdown at headings, code at function/class boundaries.

Practical chunk sizes: 512–1024 tokens for technical docs; 256–512 tokens for Q&A/conversational content; by function or class for code. Use overlap of 10–20% between adjacent chunks to prevent information loss at boundaries.

Embedding and Indexing

Each chunk passes through an embedding model (Section 4.4.3), yielding 768/1024/4096-dimensional vectors that are stored in a vector database (covered in Section 3.4.4).

Vector database selection criteria: scale (pgvector/Chroma sufficient for millions; Milvus/Qdrant for hundreds of millions); filtering (Qdrant and Milvus are strong on metadata filtering); deployment (LanceDB/Chroma for embedded; Milvus/Qdrant for server deployments); hybrid search (Weaviate and Qdrant support natively).

Query and Retrieval

The user query passes through the same embedding model and searches the vector store for the top-K (typically 3–10) nearest neighbors. The raw text and metadata are returned.

Prompt Construction

Typical template:

Answer the question based on the reference materials below. If the materials do not contain relevant information, say you don't know.

[Reference Materials]
[1] {chunk_1 text}
Source: {chunk_1 source}

[2] {chunk_2 text}
Source: {chunk_2 source}

...

[Question]
{user_query}

[Requirements]
- Cite relevant reference numbers when answering, e.g. [1]
- Answer only based on the reference materials; do not fabricate

Once assembled, the prompt is sent to the LLM to generate an answer.

Limitations of Naive RAG

It looks simple but there are many pitfalls in practice: low retrieval recall (the gap between query phrasing and document phrasing means vector similarity misses relevant content); poor ranking (the top-K includes relevant chunks but not at the top); lost cross-chunk context (related content spans multiple chunks, each of which is incomplete in isolation); hallucinated citations (the LLM invents references that don't exist); misinterpreted query intent (ambiguous queries and multi-intent queries are handled poorly).

Advanced RAG in the next section addresses each of these problems.

4.10.2Advanced RAG Techniques

Orientation: from naive RAG to production RAG — query rewriting, hybrid retrieval, reranking, and context expansion.

Query-Side Optimization

Query Expansion / Rewriting: use an LLM to first rewrite or expand the user's query into multiple variants, retrieve for each, then merge the results.

def rewrite_query(query, llm):
    prompt = f"""Rewrite the user's question below into 3 different versions,
covering possible phrasings and angles. Output only the rewritten questions, one per line.

Original question: {query}

Rewrites:"""
    return llm(prompt).strip().split('\n')

# retrieve with all variants, merge and deduplicate results
all_chunks = []
for q in rewrite_query(user_query, llm):
    all_chunks.extend(vector_db.search(q, top_k=5))

HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embedding): have the LLM first fabricate a hypothetical answer, then use that answer for retrieval — this matches more accurately than using the raw question (answers and documents share the same "answer space").

Query Decomposition: split a complex, multi-intent query into sub-queries, retrieve for each independently, then synthesize the results.

Hybrid Retrieval

Vector retrieval captures semantic similarity, but struggles with technical terms, product model numbers, and proper nouns where exact matching matters. Keyword search (BM25) excels here.

Hybrid Search: run both retrieval methods simultaneously and merge results with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF):

$\text{score}_{\text{RRF}}(d) = \sum_{r \in R} \frac{1}{k + \text{rank}_r(d)}$

$R$ is the set of rankings from different retrieval methods, $\text{rank}_r(d)$ is document $d$'s rank under method $r$, and $k$ is typically 60. Simple and blunt but empirically effective — no model training required. Weaviate, Qdrant, and Elasticsearch all support hybrid search natively.

Reranking

Stage 1 recalls top-50 (vector + BM25); Stage 2 uses a more precise model to rerank down to top-5.

Cross-encoder reranker: unlike the dual-tower design of embedding models, a reranker takes the (query, chunk) pair together and directly outputs a relevance score. 100× slower but far more accurate.

Open-source rerankers:

  • BGE-Reranker-v2 (BAAI): strong in both Chinese and English
  • Cohere Rerank: commercial, API-based
  • Jina Reranker: open-source, long-context
  • Qwen3-Reranker: new in 2024, top of leaderboards

In production RAG, reranking is the single most impactful improvement — adding a reranker typically raises retrieval accuracy from 60% to 85%+. The cost is one extra network round-trip and tens of milliseconds of latency.

Contextual Compression

Retrieved chunks can be large and wasteful to stuff into a prompt. Contextual Compression: use a small model or LLM to compress each chunk down to "the parts relevant to the query," discarding irrelevant sentences. LangChain's ContextualCompressionRetriever implements this.

Parent-Child Document Strategy

Embedding-friendly chunks (small, sentence-level) and LLM-friendly chunks (large, paragraph-level) are in tension. The solution is a two-level structure — use small chunks for retrieval, then return the large parent chunk to the LLM once a match is found.

chunks = {}   # chunk_id  → {"text": ..., "parent_id": ...}
parents = {}  # parent_id → large_chunk_text

# at retrieval time
top_chunks = vector_db.search(query, top_k=10)
parent_ids = set(c.parent_id for c in top_chunks)
context = [parents[pid] for pid in parent_ids]   # parent chunks are sent to the LLM

Sentence Window

A variant: index one vector per sentence, but when a sentence is retrieved, return N sentences before and after it together to the LLM. This achieves both retrieval precision and contextual completeness.

Self-Correction: Corrective RAG

CRAG (2024): after retrieval, an evaluator model judges whether the retrieved results are truly relevant. If not → trigger broader retrieval or fall back to the LLM's own knowledge.

Self-RAG

More aggressive: let the LLM itself decide when to retrieve, what to retrieve, and how to use the results — special tokens (Retrieve / Relevant / Support) give the model a form of "metacognition." Requires dedicated fine-tuning.

Evaluation

RAG is hard to evaluate. The popular framework RAGAS scores across several dimensions:

  • Faithfulness: whether the answer is grounded in the given context (no hallucination)
  • Answer Relevancy: whether the answer actually addresses the question
  • Context Precision: whether the retrieved chunks are relevant
  • Context Recall: whether all the information truly needed is present in the chunks

Each dimension is evaluated automatically using LLM-as-judge. Not perfectly reliable, but far better than tuning blind.

4.10.3GraphRAG and Agentic RAG

Orientation: two frontier directions for RAG — knowledge-graph augmentation and agentification.

GraphRAG

Naive RAG retrieves by "semantic similarity" and performs poorly on two query types:

  1. Multi-hop questions: "What is the relationship between A and B?" — requires chaining multiple entities
  2. Global questions: "What are the core themes in this document set?" — requires synthesis across all documents

Microsoft's GraphRAG approach (2024):

  1. Entity extraction: use an LLM to extract entities and relations from each chunk, building a knowledge graph
  2. Community detection: apply a community-discovery algorithm (Leiden) to partition the graph into thematic communities
  3. Community summarization: generate a summary for each community (LLM summarizes all entities and relations within it)
  4. Querying: depending on question type, retrieve a combination of relevant chunks / entities / community summaries to feed the LLM

Benefits: multi-hop reasoning, global synthesis, explainability (can point to "reasoning based on this edge in the knowledge graph").

Cost: high construction overhead — for a document corpus of hundreds of thousands of chunks, entity extraction alone can cost thousands of dollars in LLM calls. Only suitable for scenarios where the document base is relatively stable and queries are high-value (financial research reports, medical literature, legal compliance).

LightRAG and Variants

Microsoft GraphRAG is too heavy. LightRAG (2024) takes a lighter approach: dual-layer indexing (entity-level and relation-level), incremental updates, and graph traversal only at query time. It has gained popularity in the open-source community.

Neo4j + LlamaIndex also offers a graph RAG integration at a higher level of maturity.

Agentic RAG

Naive RAG is "one retrieval, one answer." Agentic RAG: the LLM itself decides when, how, and how many times to retrieve.

flowchart TB Q[Query] --> LLM[LLM Agent] LLM --> DEC{Decide} DEC -->|answer directly| ANS[Answer] DEC -->|retrieve| RET[Retrieve] RET --> CTX[Context] CTX --> LLM DEC -->|multiple subtasks| PLAN[Plan Subtasks] PLAN --> SUB[Subtask] SUB --> LLM

Typical patterns:

  • ReAct RAG: the ReAct loop from Section 4.11.1 applied to RAG — the LLM outputs "Search: xxx", executes it, gets the results, and continues
  • Self-Ask: the LLM breaks a complex question into sub-questions, retrieves and answers each independently, then synthesizes
  • Adaptive RAG: answer simple questions directly; use multi-round retrieval for complex ones

Combining HyDE + Agentic

Advanced production RAG systems typically blend multiple techniques: query rewriting → multi-path recall (vector + BM25) → reranking → if confidence is low, the LLM triggers a second retrieval → final generation. LangChain and LlamaIndex provide composable tooling, or you can wire it yourself.

When Not to Use RAG

RAG is not a universal key. These scenarios see limited benefit or outright degradation:

  • - Reasoning tasks (math, code): knowledge is not the bottleneck — reasoning is
  • - Creative tasks: free expression is required; retrieval constraints actually hinder it
  • - Highly dynamic / personalized: a knowledge base is irrelevant; fine-tuning is more appropriate
  • - Short factual Q&A where precise sourcing is unnecessary: the model's own knowledge is usually sufficient
PART ELEVEN

4.11 Agent

LLMs calling external tools and planning autonomously — the shift from "generating text" to "getting things done."

4.11.1From Prompt to ReAct

Orientation: the intellectual roots of agents — from CoT to ReAct to the modern agent loop.

Chain-of-Thought

CoT (Wei et al. 2022) is the first step toward the agent paradigm. The core finding: prompting LLMs to "think step by step" (adding "Let's think step by step") improves accuracy over directly giving answers, especially on math and logical reasoning tasks.

The intuition: each token generation in an LLM is a fixed-cost forward pass. Complex problems require multi-step reasoning; asking for a direct answer means completing that reasoning in a single forward pass — insufficient compute. Letting the model output its reasoning process means one forward pass per step, with total compute scaling linearly with reasoning length.

CoT provides little benefit in small models — the model must be large enough (~60B+) for significant gains. This is part of the emergence phenomenon.

Self-Consistency

CoT + sample multiple reasoning chains + majority vote. The model generates several reasoning paths for the same question; the most consistent answer wins. This corrects errors in individual chains. The cost is N× inference.

Tree of Thoughts / Graph of Thoughts

A step further — instead of a linear reasoning chain, reasoning is expanded as a tree or graph, with each node as an intermediate reasoning state, explored via search algorithms (BFS, DFS, beam search).

Suitable for planning problems (Game of 24, word puzzles). High engineering cost, rarely used in industry — CoT + self-consistency is usually sufficient.

ReAct

Reasoning + Acting (Yao et al. 2022). The key insight: make the LLM's "reasoning" more than just text — have it alternate between thinking and acting, where actions are calls to external tools.

The ReAct loop:

flowchart LR T[Thought: What information do I need?] --> A[Action: call tool] A --> O[Observation: tool result] O --> T2[Thought: next step based on result] T2 --> A2[Action: ...] A2 --> FIN[Action: finish + Answer]

Example prompt format:

Question: Who won the 2024 F1 World Championship? How old is he?

Thought: I need to find the 2024 F1 World Champion.
Action: search("2024 F1 world champion")
Observation: Max Verstappen won the 2024 F1 World Championship.

Thought: Now I need to find Max Verstappen's birth date.
Action: search("Max Verstappen birth date")
Observation: Max Verstappen was born on 30 September 1997.

Thought: He was 27 in 2024. I have my answer.
Action: finish("Max Verstappen, age 27")

ReAct Implementation

def run_react(question, tools, llm, max_steps=10):
    transcript = f"Question: {question}\n\n"
    for step in range(max_steps):
        # prompt the LLM to generate the next Thought and Action
        response = llm(transcript + "Thought:", stop=["Observation:"])
        transcript += "Thought:" + response
        # parse the Action
        action = parse_action(response)   # e.g. "search('xxx')"
        if action.name == "finish":
            return action.args
        # execute the tool
        result = tools[action.name](*action.args)
        transcript += f"Observation: {result}\n"
    return "Max steps reached"

Limitations of ReAct

ReAct is a classic early agent framework, but it has problems: fragile prompt formatting (the model occasionally produces output that doesn't match the format, causing parse failures); every step requires re-sending the full transcript (high token cost, slow); error accumulation (a single mistake in a long loop corrupts all subsequent steps); no structured tool interface (tools described in plain text are error-prone).

Modern Agents

ReAct has been largely superseded by Function Calling (covered in the next section) — once models natively support structured tool calls, prompt-convention workarounds are no longer needed.

However, ReAct's core idea (Thought → Action → Observation → loop) lives on — the modern agent loop is essentially this, just with an upgraded mechanism for expressing tool calls.

4.11.2Function Calling / MCP / Multi-Agent

Orientation: modern agent infrastructure — native tool calling, standard protocols, and multi-agent collaboration.

Function Calling

OpenAI introduced Function Calling in June 2023 (later unified as the Tools API): models are trained to output structured JSON function calls as part of their native format. No more relying on prompt conventions — this is API-level support.

Developers declare available tools to the model using JSON Schema:

{
  "type": "function",
  "function": {
    "name": "get_weather",
    "description": "Get current weather for a location",
    "parameters": {
      "type": "object",
      "properties": {
        "location": {"type": "string", "description": "City name"},
        "unit":     {"type": "string", "enum": ["celsius", "fahrenheit"]}
      },
      "required": ["location"]
    }
  }
}

The model's output may be ordinary text, or it may be:

{
  "tool_calls": [{
    "id": "call_123",
    "type": "function",
    "function": {
      "name": "get_weather",
      "arguments": "{\"location\": \"Paris\", \"unit\": \"celsius\"}"
    }
  }]
}

The application executes the function and returns the result to the model as a tool_message, continuing the loop.

Function Calling in Open-Source Models

Closed-source models (OpenAI, Anthropic) led early on. After 2024, open-source models caught up — LLaMA-3.1, Qwen2.5, DeepSeek, and Mistral all support function calling natively.

Training approach: large amounts of function-calling data (human-annotated or synthetic) are added during SFT, teaching the model to recognize when to call a tool and how to fill in parameters.

Benchmark: the Berkeley Function Calling Leaderboard (BFCL) is the standard — a set of tool-calling tasks that evaluates different models. Qwen2.5-72B, DeepSeek-V3, and others approach GPT-4o on BFCL.

Structured Output

Function Calling is fundamentally structured output. In recent years, Constrained Decoding has matured — enforcing compliance with a JSON Schema / regex / CFG directly at generation time.

Notable implementations:

  • Outlines: Python library enforcing JSON / regex / Pydantic schema
  • xgrammar: C++, integrated with vLLM/SGLang, fast
  • LMQL: query-language-style prompting
  • OpenAI Structured Output: API-level support

How it works: at each token position, the schema is used to dynamically compute the set of "valid next tokens," which are masked before the softmax. This guarantees 100% format compliance with almost no performance overhead.

MCP

Model Context Protocol (proposed by Anthropic in November 2024). The problem it solves: every AI application has to write its own tool-integration code — duplicated effort, hard to reuse.

MCP defines a standard protocol (JSON-RPC based), split into server and client:

  • MCP Server: a process that exposes tools, resources, and prompt templates. Can represent a file system, database, GitHub, Slack, and more
  • MCP Client: an LLM application (Claude Desktop, Cursor, etc.) that connects to one or more MCP servers and automatically gains access to their tools

Benefit: a standardized tool ecosystem — write an MCP server once, and every MCP-compatible LLM application can use it.

In 2025, the MCP ecosystem expanded explosively — from a single-vendor proposal by Anthropic to a de facto industry standard. Claude Desktop, Cursor, VS Code LLM plugins, Cline, Windsurf, Continue, and Zed all integrated. Open-source models like Qwen3 and DeepSeek V3.1 support the MCP format natively at the tokenizer level. Major SaaS platforms including GitHub, Slack, Notion, and Linear launched official MCP servers. From mid-2025 onward, "MCP-ready" became a baseline requirement for AI developer tools.

This is one of the rare "standardization success stories" in the LLM ecosystem — its impact runs deeper than OpenAI's plugins (now discontinued) or ChatGPT's GPT marketplace, because MCP is a model-agnostic protocol not tied to any single vendor.

Multi-Agent Systems

A single LLM doing everything vs. multiple LLMs dividing the work. Several patterns:

Supervisor-Worker: a primary LLM delegates subtasks to specialized worker LLMs (one for research, one for coding, one for writing) and aggregates the results. LangGraph and CrewAI champion this pattern.

Conversational Multi-Agent: multiple agents collaborate through dialogue. AutoGen (Microsoft) is the representative, where two or more agents exchange messages to complete a task (one writes code, one reviews, one tests).

Agent Mesh: loosely coupled, each agent has independent capabilities and collaborates on demand.

Multi-Agent Pitfalls

Lessons from building these in practice:

  1. Cost explosion: N agents communicating with each other multiplies token consumption
  2. Error amplification: one agent's error flows to the next, causing cascading failures
  3. Hard to debug: fault isolation requires tracing messages back and forth across agents
  4. Not necessarily better than a single powerful LLM: giving a single LLM more time to "think" (test-time compute) often outperforms multi-agent collaboration

Most production agent systems are Supervisor + a small number of specialized tools, not dozens of conversing agents. OpenAI Swarm and LangGraph's StateGraph both favor simple state-machine patterns.

Memory and Planning

Agents in long-running conversations need memory. Typical architecture:

  • Short-term: current conversation history (within the context window)
  • Mid-term: session summaries (long conversations are rolled and compressed into summaries)
  • Long-term: important facts stored in a vector store (fused with RAG)

On the planning side, ReAct is "reactive" — it decides at each step based on current state. Plan-then-Execute produces a complete plan first, then executes it — better suited for tasks with clear structure.

The Limits of Agents

Agents are heavily hyped, but as of early 2026, reliably deployable commercial agent scenarios remain relatively narrow:

  • Coding assistance: Cursor, Windsurf, Claude Code, Cline, OpenCode (open-source Claude Code alternative, 2025) — the most mature agent category
  • Data analysis: OpenAI's Data Analysis and Code Interpreter
  • Browser / screen operation: Anthropic Computer Use, Claude for Chrome (released late 2025) — giving LLMs real control over graphical interfaces marks a qualitative shift in agent capability
  • Vertical automation: financial analysis, legal compliance, customer service ticketing

"General-purpose agents" (personal assistants, all-purpose secretaries) remain far from practical in 2025–2026 — the main obstacles are error accumulation in long-horizon tasks and insufficient self-correction. In the second half of 2025, investment in this area accelerated rapidly: Google launched the Gemini Agent + Antigravity environment, and Anthropic continued polishing Claude's agent reliability. Reasoning models (R1, V3.2, Claude 4.5) offer strong long-horizon planning, forming the foundation for agent reliability.

A parallel trend is the flourishing of agent programming frameworks: LangChain and LlamaIndex keep evolving, and 2025 saw more "code-first" frameworks emerge (DSPy, Pydantic AI, Mastra, smolagents), turning agent orchestration into a more explicit engineering problem rather than relying solely on prompt conventions.

4.12 Multimodal

LLMs that see and hear — the expansion from "language model" to "foundation model."

4.12.1VLM Architecture

Orientation: Vision-Language Models — the main architectural approaches for connecting images to LLMs.

Three Architectural Approaches

The core problem in VLMs (Vision-Language Models): how do you feed image input to an LLM? Three main approaches:

1. Projection Adapter: the image passes through a standalone visual encoder (e.g., CLIP ViT) to produce image feature vectors, which are then mapped into the LLM's token space via a lightweight projection layer (MLP or Q-Former) and fed in alongside text tokens. Representatives: LLaVA, MiniGPT, BLIP-2. Advantage: cheap to train (the LLM is frozen; only the projection is trained). Disadvantage: information loss from compressing visual features.

2. Cross-Attention: visual features are not converted into tokens; instead they serve as the K and V of additional cross-attention layers added at each LLM layer, letting text tokens "look at" the image via cross-attention. Representatives: Flamingo, IDEFICS. Advantage: visual features retain high resolution. Disadvantage: modifies the LLM architecture, making training more complex.

3. Unified Transformer: image patches and text tokens are mixed together from the start and processed jointly by a single Transformer with self-attention throughout — no separate "visual encoder." Representatives: Fuyu (Adept), native multimodal Gemini, Chameleon (Meta). Advantage: no information loss, scales well. Disadvantage: requires training from scratch with large data and compute.

LLaVA Architecture in Detail

LLaVA (Haotian Liu 2023) is the canonical open-source multimodal model, with a clean, simple architecture:

flowchart LR IMG[Image 336x336] --> VE[CLIP ViT-L/14] VE --> IF[Image Features 576 tokens] IF --> PROJ[MLP Projector] PROJ --> VT[Visual Tokens] TXT[Text tokens] --> LLM[LLaMA / Vicuna] VT --> LLM LLM --> OUT[Text output]

Training has two stages:

  1. Feature alignment: freeze the ViT and the LLM; train only the projector. Uses image-text pair data (image + caption). The goal is to teach the projector to map visual features into a space the LLM can "understand."
  2. Instruction fine-tuning: unfreeze the LLM and train the full pipeline on multimodal instruction data (image + question + answer).

Total training cost is only a few thousand dollars — far cheaper than training a vision model from scratch.

Improved Variants

LLaVA-1.5: better data, MLP replacing the single linear layer. Significant improvement.

LLaVA-NeXT: the image is split into multiple 336×336 patches that are encoded separately, enabling high-resolution support.

Qwen-VL / Qwen2-VL: dynamic resolution (no need to scale to a fixed size), with video support. Qwen2-VL topped the open-source VLM leaderboards in 2024.

InternVL2: open-sourced by SenseTime, strong capabilities, available in multiple sizes.

High-Resolution Challenges

At low resolution (336×336), VLMs cannot read text or see fine details. Two approaches to high resolution:

  1. Patch splitting: slice the large image into sub-images, encode each independently, then concatenate the features. LLaVA-NeXT's approach.
  2. Dynamic resolution: the visual encoder accepts arbitrary input sizes. Qwen2-VL's Naive Dynamic Resolution achieves this.

Cost: more visual tokens (a 2K image can produce 2000+ tokens) crowds the context window and slows inference.

4.12.2Audio, Video, and Unified Multimodal

Orientation: beyond images and text — speech, video, and the trend toward "one model for all modalities."

Speech Understanding

Traditional ASR (automatic speech recognition) is a standalone task: a dedicated model (Whisper is the canonical example) converts speech to text, which is then sent to the LLM. This "cascade" has two problems: ASR errors propagate downstream; and information like emotion and tone in the speech is lost.

The new approach is to connect audio directly to the LLM. Similar to the VLM architecture:

  1. Audio passes through an acoustic encoder (e.g., Whisper encoder) to produce features
  2. A projection layer maps features to the LLM's token space
  3. Features are fed into the LLM alongside text tokens

Notable open-source implementations: Qwen2-Audio, LLaMA-Omni, SALMONN, Whisper + Vicuna combinations.

Speech Synthesis and Full-Duplex

Traditional speech generation (TTS) is also a standalone task (Tacotron, VITS, XTTS). The emerging trend: LLMs that directly output audio tokens.

Neural audio codecs like SNAC, EnCodec, and SoundStream compress audio into discrete token sequences (roughly 50–100 tokens per second). An LLM can generate these tokens just as it generates text, and a decoder reconstructs the waveform.

Representatives: OpenAI GPT-4o (voice mode, claimed to be end-to-end), Fish Speech, LLaMA-Omni, Moshi (Kyutai, open-source end-to-end voice conversation).

The critical metric is latency — the traditional ASR + LLM + TTS cascade introduces 1–3 seconds of delay; end-to-end LLMs can achieve under 300ms, which is what natural conversation actually requires.

Video Understanding

Video = image sequence + audio. The challenge is the temporal dimension — a 1-minute video at 30fps has 1800 frames; sending all of them is prohibitively expensive.

Mainstream strategies:

  1. Uniform sampling: take one frame every N frames. Crude but sufficient for most scenarios.
  2. Key-frame extraction: use traditional CV (scene-cut detection) or an LLM to select frames with "high information density."
  3. Spatiotemporal modeling: dedicated video encoders (VideoMAE, Video-LLaMA) encode adjacent frames together.
  4. Long context windows: Gemini 1.5's 1M-token window can accommodate large numbers of frames.

Open-source representatives: Video-LLaMA, VideoChat, LLaVA-Video, Qwen2-VL (with video input support).

Image Generation

Strictly speaking this is outside the "LLM" category, but it is routinely discussed alongside LLMs in modern multimodal frameworks. Mainstream image generation architectures:

  • Diffusion models (dominant): Stable Diffusion, SDXL, SD3, FLUX, DALL-E 3. See Chapter 3, Section 4.4.4.
  • Autoregressive: images are split into patch tokens and generated like text. Parti, LLaMA-Gen, Emu3, and Chameleon are examples. Naturally integrates with LLMs, but quality currently lags behind diffusion.
  • Hybrid: an LLM generates a latent; a diffusion decoder renders it. The approach taken by Transfusion (Meta) and Janus (DeepSeek).

A clear trend: understanding and generation unified — a single model that can both read images and draw them. DeepSeek Janus-Pro and OmniGen are the open-source representatives.

Video Generation

Major breakthrough in 2024: OpenAI Sora, Google Veo, Runway Gen-3, Kling — capable of generating high-quality videos several minutes long. The underlying technology remains diffusion models + DiT (Diffusion Transformer) architecture, treating video as spatiotemporal patches.

Open-source is catching up: CogVideoX (Tsinghua + Zhipu), Open-Sora (version 2.0 reached commercial-grade quality at just US$200K training cost), HunyuanVideo (Tencent), Mochi, LTX-Video. Quality is approaching closed-source, but the compute barrier remains high (a single training run takes hundreds of GPU-days).

A significant pivot: on 2026.3.24, OpenAI announced the shutdown of the entire Sora product line, redirecting resources toward robotic world simulation. Sora 2 (released 2025.12) had supported 25-second generation and native audio sync, but commercial adoption fell short of expectations. The torch passed to Kling 3.0 (native 4K 60fps broadcast-grade quality), Google Veo 3.1, and Seedance 1.5 Pro as the leading video generation platforms.

Unified Multimodal

The frontier direction: Any-to-Any — a single model that supports understanding and generation across text, images, audio, and video.

Representatives:

  • Gemini 2.0 (closed-source): claims native multimodality
  • GPT-4o (closed-source): unified text / speech / image
  • Chameleon (Meta, open-source): unified text + image token space
  • Emu3 (BAAI, open-source): a single model for multimodal generation
  • AnyGPT: four modalities (text, image, audio, video) end-to-end

The technical core is "converting all modalities into discrete tokens" — images use VQ-VAE, audio uses neural codecs, video uses quantized spatiotemporal patches. A single Transformer then models all of them together.

Evaluating Multimodal Models

Multimodal evaluation is harder than single-modality — there are no standard labels. Common benchmarks:

  • MMBench, MMMU, MMLU-Pro: overall multimodal capability
  • ChartQA, DocVQA: document and chart understanding
  • MVBench, VideoMME: video understanding
  • GenEval, T2I-CompBench: image generation
  • VBench: video generation

These benchmarks are also evolving rapidly — model capabilities grow so fast that old benchmarks saturate within months.

The Future of Multimodal

Three directions worth watching:

  1. Real-time performance: end-to-end voice interaction, real-time visual understanding (AR glasses on phones) — latency must drop to the 100ms range
  2. Long video: understanding full-length films, hours-long meetings — requires efficient video encoding and long context
  3. Embodied intelligence: multimodal + physical-world action — robotics, autonomous driving. VLA (Vision-Language-Action) models are the new paradigm

From 2025 onward, the boundaries of "LLM" are increasingly blurred as these models become the core of "foundation models" — with language as just one modality among many.
